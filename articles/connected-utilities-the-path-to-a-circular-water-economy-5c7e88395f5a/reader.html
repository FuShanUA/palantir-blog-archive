Connected Utilities: The path to a circular water economy

2022-01-26 · Original →

As the climate shifts, populations grow, and the standard of living expectations increase, clean water is an increasingly precious natural resource. The developed world takes clean running water for granted, and to sustain this, the industry must answer the ever-louder calls to achieve a circular economy.

Minimising waste and raw inputs implies a finely optimised, closed-loop supply chain. This in turn requires a truly connected utility provider. Let’s look at the compelling reasons why the water industry would benefit from hyper-optimised operations and data integration.

  • Net Zero is a data integration problem. And so is a fine-tuned supply chain. Ensuring that inputs and outputs are in harmony at every step of the closed-loop supply chain requires tracking flows at a granular scale. Granular detail enables granular decisions.
  • Providing a high quality customer experience has never been more important. Historically, water utilities served properties. Today, they serve the modern consumer accustomed to hyper-customer-focused service. Great customer experience touches on everything from burst mains to accurate billing and nuanced education around sustainable consumption. Once again, progress requires a deeply connected approach.
  • A circular supply chain demands a data operating system built for collaboration. In the current world, operations don’t map neatly to the water industry’s delivery targets (known in the UK sector as Outcome Delivery Incentives, or ODIs), which in turn don’t always map to budgets. The result is that collaboration is blocked, innovation is stifled and improvement potential falls through the cracks. The problem compounds when pursuing a circular supply chain.
  • Digitisation can address the challenges of dealing with some of the world’s oldest civil infrastructure. Water utilities are responsible for operating critical subterranean infrastructure, which, in some cases (e.g., London’s sewer network) dates back to the 19th century. Building a digital twin of this infrastructure would enable visibility and empower collaboration across every aspect of the network.

Successful data integration is a vital tool in the race to save the planet

Achieving a circular economy in the water industry is the promised land of sustainability and achieving Net Zero targets. This approach would reduce pressure on finite and in-demand natural resources, as well as minimise and repurpose waste. Companies who can harness the power of connectivity will also be more empowered to meet their ODIs.

Water utilities are responsible for complex engineering processes spread across vast swathes of the country. These processes range from:

  • energy intensive (e.g. pumping),
  • direct emitters (e.g. activated sludge processes emitting nitrous oxide),
  • net positive (e.g. bio-methane to electricity) and finally,
  • processes that could go either way like vehicle fleets (e.g Electric Vehicles).

Net Zero is a data integration problem — meaningful decisions are those that are made on top of a granular digital twin of a water company’s enterprise. They are made with full view of the trade-offs and the downstream impacts, and with carbon intensity accounted for at the atomic level of activity. A connected water utility uses its digital twin to investigate trade-offs and simulate what-if scenarios. This is a particularly powerful tool used in risk evaluation, for example in assessing, mitigating and preparing for service disruption during severe weather.

Driving customer experience within a connected company

From a consumer perspective, people expect water suppliers to provide them with clean water, manage, repurpose and minimise waste, and provide exceptional customer service. Compared to other household costs, we are generally less conscious of water on a use-by-use basis. We turn on the tap and it flows freely, almost always.

For water utilities, this can mean that customer interactions are minimal, but frequently negative. Consumers typically only make contact when something is wrong. This is a significant challenge for water companies who must prioritise positive customer feedback or risk regulatory penalisation.

The solution requires water providers to pursue a connected utility vision. Ideally, customer-focused decisions would be analysis-driven, referencing all of the relevant data, and decisions themselves are valued for the data points they represent. A connected utility could rapidly notify customers about potential supply interruptions, whilst having all of the necessary information instantly at hand to drive a resolution. Contact centre staff and operators share a common source of truth presented in holistic, flexible apps that reflect their world, not the underlying IT.

Collaboration is key

Building a connected utility requires that data is accessible to operational users across the business in a framework that reflects their world. It goes without saying that unfettered access to all data is not the answer. Collaboration (within an organisation and between organisations) is built on trust and security — granular security that reflects the purpose of the data usage.

A connected water utility which achieves the circular ambition will harness huge volumes of data. This data will describe in near-realtime the entire asset base from reservoir to meter and from sewer to treatment plant. They will also describe–crucially–the customer that sits in the centre and their relationship to the assets and asset events. Success means wrangling legacy systems and harmonising them with modern ERP and CRMs within a single operating system that drives data-driven decision making.

Digital Rome won’t be built in a day

Digitalising asset data in pursuit of a digital twin is a challenge for modern assets that reside above the earth’s surface, let alone for the vast networks of pipes and sewers underground. With subterranean assets, the challenges can be as fundamental as understanding if the asset is where we think it is. What this amounts to is a data quality problem (on a potentially huge scale), and what it calls for is software which empowers operators and technicians in the field to feed their observations securely back in to the digital twin, improving it as they go. This software should be seamless parsing these suggested improvements and writing back validated adjustments to vital operational systems.

In order to reach the goal of a circular economy, water companies need to be bold and commit to being fully-integrated organisations, one where operational silos are torn down and replaced with optimised operations.
