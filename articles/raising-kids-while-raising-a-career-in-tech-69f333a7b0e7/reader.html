Raising Kids While Raising a Career in Tech

2022-05-26 · Original →

I’m Marina Miller, and I’m one of the Group Leads of the Production Infrastructure department. Since joining Palantir in 2012, it’s been a wild ride both as a growing leader in tech and as a mother. There were many hills to climb and moments of doubt. Among them, there were also opportunities to take on something impactful, something hard, and something that I wasn’t sure I would succeed at, especially with kids. And yet, today and throughout my career as working mother, I have a seat at the table. My ideas are heard and I feel excited bringing my strengths to my group, my colleagues. I am thriving as both a leader and a working mother.

My journey may look different from yours, and while Palantir gave me the support and latitude to “have it all” — career growth and being the parent I want to be, I know this unfortunately isn’t the case for all working mothers. I had to have uncomfortable conversations and lean on outstanding colleagues. I’ve certainly benefited from inherent privileges that I will not attempt to disguise as anything but luck. I hope my story and the lessons I’ve learned serve as encouragement and support for your growth, wherever you may be.

Becoming a working mom

Transitioning to life as a working mom was scary and filled with doubts. That’s completely natural. Colleagues should be there to both affirm concerns you may have, and support you through addressing them.

When I joined Palantir almost a decade ago, I took on a role as a Deployment Strategist. This included traveling almost 100% of the time, because I wanted to work in our government space in DC while staying close to family in New Jersey. I was (and still am!) incredibly motivated by our mission — at the time I got to work with some of the largest and most critical US government institutions that were using Palantir’s Gotham platform on high-profile terrorism cases and solving mortgage-backed securities fraud rings. It was exhilarating, and I felt my personal mark on making our world a better and safer place.

In 2016, I gave birth to my son and took over 20 weeks of fully paid maternity leave. It was a true maternity leave — I didn’t have to hop on work calls, and instead spent the full 20 weeks both enjoying my newborn and adjusting to the challenges of being a new mother. Yet as my maternity leave came to a close, some doubt began to creep in. I began to think through how being a mother would affect my impact and growth in my career. Wanting to be home, I knew I wouldn’t be able to travel for government work. Would I be sidelined to a project that didn’t excite me? Would I still have access to the same growth I experienced over the past 4 years? Would I be able to find my footing with colleagues outside the government space?

Fortunately, my doubts were quickly mitigated; on my first day back, my lead at the time (Keyur P. also a parent), enthusiastically asked 100 questions about baby sleep, balancing time with my spouse, and silly baby sounds. At the same time, he acknowledged my fears and encouraged me to talk to people working on different projects at Palantir. He reminded me that my strengths and skillsets, my goal orientation, human EQ, and execution power, were transferrable to many problems the company worked on. He gave me confidence and reminded me to be open to opportunities that may seem outside my comfort zone, that would continue to accelerate my growth instead of stifling it.

That opportunity came in the form of a colleague encouraging me to take on a role as the head of our internal data team. I went from having doubts of finding my footing again to suddenly leading an organization. It was completely out of my comfort zone and again I wondered, could I learn and excel at what felt like a completely new area? Would I be able to focus when I was sleep deprived, missing my baby and reluctantly leaving him with a new caregiver?

Support + Discovery = Growth

Learning to be a working mother, while at the same time learning to build a team, presented challenges. Being supported by Palantir meant being supported in both pursuits — not only having my needs as a mother met, but also being fully empowered to pursue new growth opportunities.

Though having affirmation from my team was critically important, feeling supported by the company was priceless. I was welcomed by other mothers at Palantir and was set up with brand new pumping equipment, as well as a beautiful purpose-built mother’s room. I was encouraged to set up my calendar with pumping breaks, time commuting, and “baby time.” Every single person up to the executive level respected those blocks. So far, so good!

As I successfully got more and more responsibility in my role leading the data team, I found myself learning things I never imagined I was capable of — I was working on teaching our internal teams (across finance, recruiting, events, and more) to leverage Foundry for internal company operations while also successfully leading a group of very technical data engineers, despite not being an engineer myself. I grew my capacity to be a leader for my team. I mentored employees that felt they had stagnated in their careers to be more impactful. I did all of that while still managing to get home to my son and spend quality time with him every day.

As the success of this team grew and took off, Palantir asked me to speak at our company-wide Hobbitcon event on some of the compelling success our team was bringing to the company. This was an exciting opportunity to share the impactful work my team was doing. However, Hobbitcon was being held in California, while my spouse would be on work-related travel. Rather than taking the opportunity away, Palantir helped accommodate my talk to be a remote one, with my face broadcast on a large screen to a live audience. It was the first time I felt confident it was possible to both grow in my career and be the present parent I strived to be.

Later, I decided I was ready to travel again — occasionally to our London and Palo Alto offices to work and connect with various team members. Again, there were a lot of logistical questions on how I could do this between childcare and pumping. Palantir provided the Milk Stork service to me at no cost to make the process as seamless as possible. Being supported was more than the services and resources, but how they were put in place to help fuel my growth!

Realizing my growth was exponential

Being supported as a mother enabled me to realize my potential and grow my team. My growth in turn ended up being great for the business!

From here, a trusted colleague (the same one that encouraged me to join the data team!) asked me to take on a new, more complicated team — our cloud team. This team was bigger than the data team, and the subject matter was completely foreign to me. I was also nervous, because I was pregnant with my second child, and wasn’t sure if the team would want someone that would be out on leave in 9 months. I openly shared this with my colleague, who was thrilled by the news and more invested than ever in my success. Palantir again supported me by ensuring I could take an AWS course and exam to learn as quickly as I could. My lead and mentors continuously reminded me and encouraged me that I had the skills to succeed here, and gave me the confidence to jump in and execute. I did it, and this time I realized there was not a ceiling to what I could do and to how much growth Palantir was willing to invest in for my career.

When my daughter was born in 2018, upon return from my second maternity leave and almost 6 months later, I was again nervous. Would I return to the same team, where one of my direct reports was running the team in my place in the interim? How would I again have an impact even bigger than before while juggling two kids?

Quelling my fears almost instantly, two group leads of the Production Infrastructure department, Greg D. and Brian K. (appreciate you both!), invited me to help scale their organization — leveraging the same skills they saw me leverage with previous teams. Upon the encouragement of my new lead, Maggie A. (not a parent, but also a woman who was deeply invested in my growth) I accepted their proposal under the condition I would be promoted to a group lead along with them — and they said yes! As I write about this experience in hindsight, it feels like I had the unwavering support of my lead and colleagues.

That said, it’s important to note this portion of my career required multiple “uncomfortable” conversations. I had to advocate for myself, create a role that did not exist for other non-engineers at the time, and really put myself out there on behalf of my career and parenting goals. These moments tested my self-confidence, and I often felt my heart racing outside of my body as I forced myself to speak up. Success here truly required a partnership — internally with myself along with trusted mentors who believed in me.

The journey from here was wild. Together we grew the department from 30 people to over 100. I took on recruiting, resourcing and various operational challenges I had never experienced. To date, my role as a group lead in Production Infrastructure sometimes feels like running a startup — the access to problems, the opportunity to innovate and learn the infrastructure space has been incredible. Doing it successfully while raising two small humans (ages 6 and 3 at the time of writing) during COVID is not something I could have done at any other company.

Finding a community

In addition to my growth, I’ve also developed a strong network of other mothers that are leaders at Palantir. The head of our major internal operations team, the head of our commercial business and more. All of these women are part of Palantir’s mother affinity group and are both official and unofficial resources to me. We often connect on existing challenges we are experiencing — how to balance a COVID quarantine with a critical business deadline, how to best use Palantir’s care.com benefit to find a new caregiver, and how to support other new working moms at Palantir. Because of how valuable this network has been to me, I’ve also in turn invested in ensuring such a network exists in Production Infrastructure. When I started in the org in 2018, I was one of three women in the department, and the only mother. As of January 2022, the department has 15 women, most of which are leading teams — including another working mom and parents on every team.

Palantir has certainly come a long way in how we take care of our parents. There were certainly road bumps and lessons along the way, but I’m proud of where we’ve ended up, and the direction we’re taking forward. The tech industry as a whole is still undergoing that journey, as businesses continue to (hopefully) learn and improve on how to provide for their employees. I, for one, am looking forward to having more working mothers on our in Production Infrastructure organization, as well as growing the parent community at Palantir. Check out our open roles here and join me if what I’ve shared is something you care about too.
