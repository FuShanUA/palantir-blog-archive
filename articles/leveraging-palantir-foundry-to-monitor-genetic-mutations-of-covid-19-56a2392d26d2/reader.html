Leveraging Palantir Foundry to Monitor COVID-19 Genetic Mutations

2021-09-07 · Original →

As the world continues to respond to the SARS-CoV-2 (COVID-19) pandemic, scientific institutions and public health officials worldwide are grappling with new genetic variants of the virus. Viruses, by their nature, are constantly changing, and the variants that evolve can have different characteristics. Some mutations make the virus more benign while others make it more transmissible (allowing the virus to spread quicker), more virulent (leading to more severe disease or higher mortality rates), or more resistant to the body’s defense system (evading the body’s normal defense systems, including vaccine-induced immunity). Therefore, it is crucial for scientists to sequence the genome of viruses isolated from patient samples to understand better the extent to which new COVID cases are the result of local transmission or are imported, and the pace at which the virus acquires new genetic mutations.

Sequencing in a Pandemic: Expedience and Clarity Matter

The discovery of new variants often takes place in laboratories with several sequencing machines that share data with bioinformaticians, whose analysis is performed in silos using high-performance computing (HPCs) environments in a non-distributed and non-scalable way. This process works well with low data volumes and batch processing. However, in a global pandemic, with the threat of more virulent or transmissible strains, scientific discovery requires greater transparency and more expedient collaboration. Scientists must be able to sequence as many samples as possible as quickly as possible. By tracking these new strains, scientists can ensure vaccines remain effective against newly evolving viruses and, when necessary, develop new vaccines. Their findings are crucial to inform appropriate public health action. It is imperative in such health crises for government and public health officials to draw on that variant data to make informed decisions, including actions that limit the spread of new variants.

While genomic data can be represented in several different formats, we will focus on the most common: FASTA and FASTQ format files.

Understanding Genomic Monitoring

Usually, when we think about genomic data, we think about gigantic strings with four characters (A, C, T, and G), gigabytes (or terabytes, or even petabytes) of data. Each character represents a single nucleotide that binds to its base pair to make a double-stranded DNA through hydrogen bonding. The size of the string varies by organism; genome sizes of bacteriophages and viruses, for instance, range from about 2,000 base pairs to over 1 million base pairs, whereas human genomes contain over 3 billion base pairs [1]. In bioinformatics, FASTA format files are text-based formats used to represent the nucleotide sequences that characterize genomes, essentially storing the string sequence.

In nature, virus genomes constantly mutate and evolve as they continue to divide and spread in host organisms. Efficiently tracking these changes — collecting, sequencing, and analyzing viral samples over time — helps bioinformaticians determine how genomes have deviated from their original sequence. Modern sequencing machines can’t read genomes from beginning to end; instead, they read strips of data, assign quality scores, and store the data in a FASTQ format file.

Foundry and the De Novo Assembly Approach

A genome can be assembled without knowing the likely sequence of DNA fragments from a ‘reference’ genome. Known as the ‘de novo assembly’ approach, it is performed in an HPC environment requiring significant compute power to execute. Traditionally, sequencers generate FASTQ format files, which are then transferred to HPC environments, where workflow languages are used to coordinate de novo assembly. This well established approach, on its own, has limitations; it doesn’t foster transparency, accountability, or automation. Such limitations, which are disadvantageous when looking for a fast and scalable solution during a pandemic, can be remedied with Foundry.

Foundry has demonstrated its value to scientists and bioinformaticians who use this approach by easing automation while encouraging collaboration, transparency, and accountability in biomedical data handling. Foundry’s external compute module connector leverages the popular workload management system Slurm and other open-source tools to connect with high-performance computing nodes. By managing all the data and code versioning, while taking care of pipeline schedules validation and health checks, Foundry frees scientists and bioinformaticians from managing those processes. Instead, it focuses them on core genomic data analysis, research, and outcomes.

Foundry and Map-based Reference Genome Sequencing

In other cases, genomic sequences can be mapped to a reference strain when an organism’s fully sequenced genome in FASTA format is known. Typically accomplished in an HPC environment, scientists who run map-based reference genome sequencing using Foundry’s Genome Alignment Module have the added benefits of cloud elasticity to scale their pipeline in a fully automated way. This capability means that genomes can be aligned in a cluster of computing resources that can grow and shrink in size depending on demand without requiring additional pipeline changes. Palantir partners engaged in the COVID-19 response have highlighted the usefulness of such elasticity, particularly when there was a surge in need for viral genomic alignment to distinguish and track COVID-19 variants.
