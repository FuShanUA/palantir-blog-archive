Having access to the right data at the right time to make the right decision is absolutely critical to modern enterprises. Data improves resilience to macro-shocks and can help businesses adjust with agility to changing market dynamics — to not only survive, but thrive in times of uncertainty. If organizations could unlock and operationalize this data, they could make better decisions, find opportunities for cost savings, save on resources, and in some cases, avoid massive waste in operational spending.

Historically, the most accessible way of doing this was by employing an ETL (Extract, Transform and Load), such as Informatica or open-source on Apache Nifi. ETLs fit between raw data sources and an operational data warehouse, extracting data from these sources, performing routine, manual transformations and depositing the results into a repository — which, in turn, fed visualization tools like Power BI or Tableau.

What’s an ETL?

Extraction is the art of bringing data into a system. This can often be the most time-consuming step. The ETL must be manually programmed and reprogrammed to integrate data from hundreds of different legacy systems, file types and data structuring paradigms. Some organizations have millions of tables to extract.

Transform involves cleansing the data, sorting the data, and combining it into a logical framework. This can be intensive for data engineers since they are tasked with custom-coding a lot of these transformations and applying unique rules to check for quality.

Load is the final step where the ETL takes that transformed data and loads it into its end destination . Load can be completed in batches or all at once. The amount of information you are loading will determine how much time and compute this process takes.

Common ETL Use-Cases

Marketing and Sales Data Integration — pull web analytics or salesforce data into one place to better optimize persona-level marketing and understand granular audience analytics.

Database Replication — for data resilience and redundancy, ETLs are often used to create a backup of key data in case something happens to the original

Business Intelligence — pull data from across your systems to feed BI tools and workflows, including visualization and reporting.

Ensure Data Quality- through data profiling and standardization, deduplication, data enhancement, data cleansing and verification, ETLs force organizations to take a hard look at the data living in legacy systems.

Where can it feel limited?

Extract — this step alone can take months, even years, with traditional ETLs, depending on how much data the ETL is processing. In some cases, this is millions of tables across 20–30 different systems.

Transform — Because transformations must be often be custom-coded and validated by software engineers or developers, ETLs can introduce significant overhead and redundant, time-consuming work for your engineering team — whose talents are better spent analyzing and operationalizing data, rather than building custom pipelines.

Load- the process ends here. You load the transformed data into an end system, but what comes next? How do you gain value from the data? An entirely separate process is needed to bring business value from the data you just ran through the ETL.
