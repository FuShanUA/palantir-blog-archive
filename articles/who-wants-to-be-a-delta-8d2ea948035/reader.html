Who Wants to be a Delta?

2021-10-21 · Original →

Editor’s Note: In this blog post, Chinemerem E. reflects on her journey from Backend Engineer at a FinTech company to a Forward Deployed Software Engineer (FDSE) at Palantir.

Finding Palantir

I joined Palantir as a Forward Deployed Software Engineer (or FDSE, also internally referred to as a Delta) in September 2020, after almost two years working as a backend Engineer at a Nigerian FinTech company. My former role involved planning and building out Application Programming Interfaces (APIs) and infrastructure for various features on a payment gateway application. Though the technical work was exciting, I realized I wanted to solve challenging problems beyond finance and to do so, I needed to find a new opportunity.

Around the time I started my job search, I heard about Palantir from a friend. Honestly, it couldn’t have been a better match. The company, itself impressive, was doing exciting work with real impact across various industries. But the icing on the cake for me was the Forward Deployed Software Engineer role. A position where I could build right next to the customer, build for more than one customer, and apply myself in areas outside core software development? Count me in. I’ll start tomorrow!

Becoming a Palantirian

I onboarded in the midst of a global pandemic. The onboarding team did a fantastic job easing my cohort into Palantir via a completely virtual environment. But as the excitement of joining the best software company in the world (yes, I said it) slowly faded, I started to see just how different the FDSE role is, and that I needed a new perspective in order to be successful.

Initially, I tried to cling to how my past job and the FDSE role were similar but doing so was holding me back from realizing my full potential. I had a breakthrough when I finally accepted that the Delta role is simply different from what I’d known. I was now a Forward Deployed Software Engineer and didn’t need to bend my new reality to fit the traditional software engineering role. In fact, being an FDSE is its own unique role. In this blog, I share my observations and reflect on the changes I’ve made to fully embrace this new forward deployed life as a Delta.

A Different Engineering Landscape

The work that FDSEs do at Palantir can be wide ranging. For the most part, Deltas work within Foundry and Gotham, Palantir’s key platforms that offer a suite of services, applications, and application-builders that provide capabilities like data integration, privacy and security controls, interactive data analysis, dashboards and reporting, operational workflows, AI & ML infrastructure, high-frequency data streaming, and more. So far, I’ve worked primarily with Foundry, which drives the observations that follow.

As a new Delta, one of the first major adjustments I had to make concerned my engineering toolkit. I went from having multiple applications/platforms that handled the different aspects of my software engineering role to working almost entirely in one web-based platform: Foundry. I felt like I had lost something important or maybe become less of an engineer. My perception changed as I got to experience the power the Foundry platform wields. Foundry became my primary tool for solving problems. Among other things, Foundry served as my integrated development environment (IDE), database client, and testing tool. It is no small feat for one platform to house both development environment and user-facing interface, all with a very intuitive user experience! Did I already mention I work at the best software company in the world?

I also came to realize that in my new role, productivity and effectiveness are tied more to delivering business outcomes than knowing every single technical detail of the solutions I deliver. For example, if I want to set up an automatic data synchronization (sync) from a customer’s database to build an operational application, I don’t need to build out the entire data sync logic from scratch. I would simply configure the data sync application in Foundry to do the job. Having been abstracted from the technical details of the data synchronization tool, I am able to spend more time ensuring the end application truly solves the relevant business problem.

Thinking About Data Differently

“I have to think about data differently now.” I remember saying this repeatedly in my first few months as a Delta. In my previous roles, data was typically just a record of events that occurred in a system, the result of users creating or modifying an entity with the click of a button on the front-end or via an API. My primary responsibility to data had been to arrange it neatly into a relational database, making sure the database schema was as normalized as possible and that if business rules changed, the schema could easily be modified or extended.

“Data is no longer a mere record of the past but the very foundation to build and shape a desired future. I now ask myself: ‘What can I build to turn this data into actionable intelligence?’”

Over the course of my first year as a Delta, working across two regions and three industries, I began to see data differently. Data is no longer a mere record of the past but the very foundation to build and shape a desired future. I now ask myself: “What can I build to turn this data into actionable intelligence?” As a result, I am better positioned as a Delta to truly help our customers emancipate their data.

Embracing Autonomy & Taking Ownership

Prior to joining, I’d heard about Palantir’s flat, decentralized teams, each with decision-making authority, and that its people have the freedom to approach, own, and solve problems creatively. I found the prospect of that kind of autonomy at work exciting. I wasn’t sure what that freedom would look like in practice but I was hoping it would lead to a sense of fulfillment from succeeding at doing the most valuable kind of work.

It turns out that autonomy at Palantir is a function of ownership; we take responsibility for the decisions we make. And since I’ve joined Palantir, this freedom has helped me find that sense of fulfillment I’d been seeking. Over the course of my first year at Palantir, I learned to be brave, to not shy away from unfamiliar situations, to embrace the dynamism of the Delta role that is both enticing and intimidating, and consequently to own more aspects of the work required to drive the outcomes I want for our customers. To illustrate how I’ve grown into being a Delta, it’s helpful to consider how the Forward Deployed Software Engineer role differs from a traditional Software Engineer role.

At a typical software company, a software engineer’s scope of influence is just one leg in what I think of as a relay race. A handful of people (Company Executives, Product Managers, Engineering Leads, etc.) determine the product’s direction and decide which features should be built. Then, they pass the baton to the engineering team to plan, design, and build out the product features. Once built, the engineering team then passes the baton to the sales and customer support team to ensure end users are satisfied with the originally scoped product. When enough user feedback has been obtained to inform learning, this last team shares the feedback with the Product Managers, and the relay race starts again.

As a Forward Deployed Software Engineer, I’m responsible for more than just application development on a project. Deltas engage in initial customer discussions to explore, identify, and scope out the customer’s business problems. We determine how best to deploy Palantir products to solve these problems and work with our Product Development teams when new capabilities are required. We are there with the users when the applications go live and we enable the client’s IT teams to build independently on Foundry (or Gotham). By design, the Delta role is high touch and requires taking ownership, which has widened my scope of influence as an engineer.

I’ve come to realize that I have quite a bit of freedom to take my work to the next level and that typically I’m the only one who can hold myself back. At Palantir, it’s up to the individual to step up and decide to own more. I have also seen (from my experience and that of more tenured Palantirians) that growth — or a promotion, if you like — happens when a person demonstrates their increased capacity simply by owning more.

Focusing on Customer Outcomes

Deltas are first and foremost focused on customer outcomes, an aspect that appealed to me as a prospective employee as did Palantir’s wide array of customers and penchant for solving complex problems. Initially, I expected a ‘customer outcome focus’ would translate to building all kinds of cool solutions for all the cool companies in the world. While it’s true we do cool things, the real impact comes from putting the customer first, not the technology. Keeping that prioritization clear has helped me shift my focus from technical contributions to business outcomes.

My mentor in the Palantir affinity group Palnoir remarked that “one of the best parts about being an FDSE is being able to point to a real life implication of your work.” This is so true. It’s not only the most rewarding part of being a Delta but also a core responsibility. Creating impactful outcomes for customers is front of mind for me each day.

“Over the course of my first year at Palantir, I learned to be brave, to not shy away from unfamiliar situations, to embrace the dynamism of the Delta role that is both enticing and intimidating, and consequently to own more aspects of the work required to drive the outcomes I want for our customers.”

When I engage with customers to help solve their business problems, my approach centers around paying attention to the problem and showing empathy. Paying attention to the problem is clearly important so that we don’t spend time and resources building solutions that are not useful. But there is also the risk that not paying attention could mean that we only build a good solution at the expense of a solution that markedly elevates the way the customer operates. Empathy is also very important. We work with real people who have real problems that our software can solve. I have found that it is not enough to understand the problem well but to also put the customer first in determining a solution. This is how I take responsibility for the outcomes I want to see, by making sure that I am in tune with what the customer really needs and building toward that.

Let’s Change the World

I joined Palantir as an FDSE because I wanted to do more as an engineer. I wanted to work in more industries than Finance. I wanted more autonomy and the chance to have a greater impact. And here I am! Life as a Delta feels different because it is different. It has taken some getting used to but it is amazing. I am no longer showing up to work just to build an app or a platform. I show up each day to change the world. Well, at least some part of it.

So, now who wants to be a Delta? Join me.
