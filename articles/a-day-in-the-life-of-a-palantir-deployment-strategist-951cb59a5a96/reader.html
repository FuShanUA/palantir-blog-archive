A Day in the Life of a Palantir Deployment Strategist

2022-03-08 · Original →

One of the most interesting, yet potentially confusing, roles at Palantir is the Deployment Strategist, known internally as “Echo.”

At Palantir, we help companies leverage their data for a deep understanding of their business and build a digital twin of their operations. To solve our clients’ toughest problems, we often embed closely with them to design, configure and implement innovative workflows and applications.

On a typical project, you’ll find a mix of Deployment Strategists and Forward-Deployed Engineers (commonly dubbed “Deltas”). Theoretically, Echos are more product-managers while Deltas are more technical, but in reality, both roles are a mix between product manager, software engineer and strategist and the lines between the two roles blur heavily quite often. In this post, we’ll focus on the unique role of the Deployment Strategist.

Deployment Strategists run the gamut, from people who code all day, every day and rarely talk with clients to folks who never touch code but coordinate and mentor multiple projects internally and externally at the same time. In other words, it’s a pretty wide spectrum, and no two Deployment Strategists are the same. In a more traditional project setting, Deployment Strategists tend to focus on tasks like:

  • Understanding the client’s operations and figuring out where Palantir software can have the biggest impact
  • Designing and implementing user-centric workflows and applications around problems or gaps in the existing systems
  • Iterating rapidly with users to make sure the solution is what they want and need
  • Implementing and scaling developed solutions
  • Managing client relationships
  • Managing the project in terms of timelines and deliverables
  • Representing Palantir with senior client leadership
  • This list goes on…

Daily life

With so many priorities, you might be asking yourself: what does an actual day look like for a Deployment Strategist? Fortunately, dull moments are few and far between, because there’s so much variety in what you take on. Personally, I’m currently working on a pilot engagement for a large drugstore company in the US, even though I’m based in Europe. We’re working on co-creating a number of different workflows with the customer that are targeted at giving them more visibility into operations and automating certain processes, so they can focus on the challenges that really need their expertise.

Here’s a look at a typical day for me, as well as for two of my colleagues. One of them leads a more established project and is becoming an industry expert in that space, while the other works at the intersection between embedded and internal development teams, bridging the gap between what our products can do already and what is needed in the field. And yes, we all have the same job title!

Daily Life as a Pilot Lead (CO)

09:00 AM — Get my head in the game: recap the broader goals of my project, what the clients want and need, and where we are (frequently done during a run and/or accompanied by strong coffee)

10:00 AM — Daily stand-up: make sure everyone is on the same page, knows what to work on and why, and doesn’t have any blockers

10:15 AM — Continue working on a data pipeline to integrate the customer’s ERP system into an application that automatically resolves discrepancies in shipments

12:30 PM — Lunch — depending on which office you call home, head over to the canteen, venture outside, or order in, and find out what your colleagues are up to (the breadth of projects is wide and often there are interesting synergies)

01:30 PM — Iteration session with clients to show them the progress I’ve made since morning — understand their real-world problems, listen to their feedback, and nudge them to come up with new features they didn’t think were possible “just yet”

02:30 PM — Random Sync with someone else at Palantir to make sure I’m always meeting new people who work on different things than I do

02:50 PM — Moonlight on an initiative that excites me outside of my project world (I currently do outreach for Foundry for Builders, Palantir’s offering to start-ups, because I find it inspiring to see what our software can do in the hands of founders)

04:30 PM — Work on materials for an upcoming project milestone presentation to senior stakeholders, set up an internal dress rehearsal, and shoot off a quick progress update through Slack so my colleagues waking up on the other side of the world know what’s going on

Why this role

Being a Deployment Strategist, or Echo, is like taking on a rainbow role. What I’ve found fascinating about this position is how you can shape it into almost anything you want.

When I was looking for a job, a huge soft criterion was that I would still be able to learn new things that truly interest me and grow in areas that I deemed important. I wanted to improve my coding skills, even though I wasn’t very technical, and I made a point of writing code all day long until I felt somewhat satisfied with my knowledge. I also knew I wanted to become more skilled at project management and maximize the impact of my work. Palantir actively supported these goals of mine, even though from the company’s perspective, it would have made more sense to focus on my strengths and keep me far away from a code repository.

As important as developing these skills was to me, I also enjoy being hands-on and building meaningful products that others find useful. Getting to work on Palantir Foundry has given me the opportunity to create things from scratch, and Foundry makes it easy to be iterative and rapidly build up, tear down, and adapt solutions. As an Echo, I have tangible output — I’m actually designing and implementing applications that will make clients’ jobs easier and more efficient — and I get to work side-by-side with the future users instead of working in a black box. As a project lead and mentor, it’s also part of my job to witness and support my team members’ growth paths both personally and professionally, which I’m constantly humbled by. I personally enjoy context-switching, so I’ve worked on a number of 4–12 week pilot engagements to see new industries and meet new colleagues, but there are many Echos that stick to one project for a long time and immerse themselves deep into the topic while building long-lasting client relationships.

Why Palantir

Data can be a blessing and a curse — it can spark invaluable insights if you know how to use it, but if you don’t, it can prove inadequate or even harmful. When reading about the world of data, I find it frightening how often data is misused and how little people question decisions that are “backed by data.” Helping private and public organizations leverage their data in ways that are logical, ethical, efficient, and transparent is something I find not only important, but also in alignment with my personal values — and that’s what I get to work on as a Deployment Strategist.

On the outside, Palantir can seem like a black box. But on the inside, Palantir operates as a no-hierarchy company where the best idea wins, no matter your title or tenure. It’s still small (and resource-constrained) enough that you get a lot of responsibility early on. You’re constantly wearing three different hats — you’re usually responsible for running a project and building a product at the same time, while making sure all you learn flows back into Palantir’s software. You can text anyone for advice or context or examples, so you don’t have to constantly figure out tough things on your own. Palantir also does do a good job at pairing new folks with an experienced lead and mentor who can help you navigate this admittedly untraditional company and grow in the ways you want to grow.

The most challenging aspect of working at Palantir

As attractive as “no hierarchy” sounds at first, it does come with a very high assumed level of autonomy and responsibility. You frequently have to make confident decisions under uncertain conditions and set a course for an unknown future. Chances are, even though you don’t have a lot of context, it’s more than anyone else has. Constantly being under this sort of pressure can be exhausting. You will come out the other end with a strong muscle in dealing with ambiguity, but it’s still a process. Palantir’s flat hierarchy guarantees a steep learning curve.

My advice for someone considering the Deployment Strategist role

There are no defined paths to this role, no matching study backgrounds or industries, and no definitions of what it means to do well. You shape your own role as a Deployment Strategist. The more responsibility you take on, the more you get; the more opinions you form, the more people listen. Whether you want to write code all day, conduct user interviews, scale existing accounts or win over new ones, the stage is yours and you should apply your strengths and weaknesses to it in your unique way.

Just as no day in our professional life is the same, no two Deployment Strategists are the same. We are a collection of people who don’t fit into a single role description, which is why we need to constantly define (and redefine) it. We go where we’re needed most and get inherently itchy when we see problems. We are the first ones in and the last ones out, have a get-shit-done mentality, constantly challenge the existing status quo, and value execution high.

Deployment Strategists are strong at being reflective, self-driven, technical, and empathetic. You know best where your strengths and weaknesses are, and Palantir will ask you how it can get better as a company instead of molding you into a role. You see the roadblocks ahead and do what it takes to unblock yourself and your team, whether by teaching yourself a new coding language or by shouting at leadership. You truly care about understanding your users and their needs. In short, as a Deployment Strategist at Palantir, you strive to manage difficult situations and find unconventional solutions to data-centric challenges, and you do it your way — because you know best where you’re needed most.
