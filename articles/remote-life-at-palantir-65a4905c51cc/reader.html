Remote Life at Palantir: Intern and new grad perspective

2021-04-05 · Original →

Editor’s Note: As we embark on a second summer of remote work, we’re publishing posts about remote life at Palantir. Click to read Julio’s story for a deep-dive into one intern’s experience and lessons learned.

When COVID-19 forced the world into quarantine during March 2020, the Palantir intern team was in the midst of planning our annual internship program. As the pandemic grew worse and working from home progressed from two weeks to four weeks, we knew we had to pivot our program — quickly. The recruiting team banded together to figure out how we change our standard internship experience to a fully remote program.

Traditionally, our internship program is a 12-week opportunity for students to gain first-hand experience using Palantir technology to solve some of the toughest problems our engineers face on a day-to-day basis while embedding themselves in our culture by working in one of our many offices. Palantir is a place where interns are empowered to imagine and build epic things, from a new feature to a new product roadmap. From day one, interns are treated like full-time employees and encouraged to contribute — not just to the codebase, but to our strategy as well.

With the help of the intern team, recruiters, engineers, and leadership, we were able to swiftly build a replica of our traditional program in a new, remote environment. Our program kicked off with virtual onboarding, where cohorts ramped up on all things Palantir, introduced themselves to one another, and met their mentors and leads. Throughout the program, we hosted hosted social and developmental programming sessions, including spotlights on various business groups and their work, brown bag lunch discussions with leadership, social events, and “random syncs” where we matched interns up with experienced Palantir engineers.

To provide a better perspective on the the remote experience at Palantir, we’ve interviewed former interns and current new grads who’ve experienced Palantir in the virtual world.

  • Emily N. — 2020 Intern, Forward Deployed Engineer, Ohio State University
  • Ciara B. — 2019 Intern, 2020 New Grad, Forward Deployed Engineer
  • Cassidy S. — 2020 New Grad, Forward Deployed Engineer
  • Ryan A. — 2020 Intern, Software Engineer, UT Austin
  • Nick H. — 2020 Intern, Forward Deployed Engineer, UMichigan
  • Ben A. — 2019 Intern, 2020 New Grad, Forward Deployed Engineer

Tips for preparing for a remote experience at Palantir

Ben: Don’t be afraid to ask for help early and often. People are generally super eager to be helpful, and everyone understands the calculus that if they can spend 15 or 30 minutes of their time to save you half a day of spinning your wheels, it’s a huge net gain for the team as a whole. There’s a really steep learning curve for everyone that joins Palantir, but asking for help liberally is a great way to accelerate that process.

Emily: I primarily prepared for my remote internship by setting up a good working environment. I made my desk exclusively for work and kept it free of any personal distractions (ie: personal laptop, clutter, etc.). As an avid notetaker, I made sure to stock my drawers with notepads, sticky notes, and pens so that they would be within easy reach. My environment plays a huge rule in my productivity, which is why I focused so heavily on this in preparing for my internship.

Ciara: Write everything down. The good advice you get, the good bits from impromptu tech talks. It will help you absorb and retain the crazy amount of new information coming at you.

Cassidy: Be willing to block off part of your day for focus time and try to model a Maker’s Schedule. Because collaboration has to be so much more intentional in a remote world, trying to group your meetings together separate from your working time is imperative to being able to produce work and dive deeply into what you’re owning at any given time.

Building community

Emily: As an intern class, there were many different channels of connection. I decided to take a different approach to fostering relationships as I prefer one-on-one conversations, especially in a virtual setting. I made it my goal to identify those that I would have been working closely with if we were in person. In my case, I was looking for female FDEs that would have been at the DC office since they would have likely been my roommates. This gave me a starting point for reaching out to other interns with something in common. These ended up being my closest relationships.

Nick: Our intern class did all sorts of things to bond over the summer. We held virtual socials every Thursday and Friday after work, played a lot of Skribbl, worked together through a day-long puzzle-hunt, and a lot of other activities. I spent many nights last summer up late bingeing Netflix with intern friends I made during the first week of my internship. I was super grateful to make a lot of friends this past summer and it was a huge motivator to come back to Palantir.

Cassidy: Outside of my role as a Forward Deployed Engineer, I have joined the Women in Tech (WiT) Affinity Group as a global representative and helped to start a formalized socialization program for New Hires onboarding virtually. There are so many opportunities for events like trivia nights, team bonding syncs, and other sessions that can help you get to know different communities more intimately — take advantage of them!

Ben: I can’t emphasize enough how important it is to meet people, especially when you’re new! Talking to other Palantirians is really the only way of learning about and experiencing the culture, and I’m sure you’ll quickly find that the culture is one of the best things about working here. The people around you are also by far the most valuable resource for learning and getting up to speed when you’re new. If you want to ramp up quickly and effectively, the key is to figure out how to leverage the breadth of knowledge and experience that everyone around you has. Don’t limit yourself to meeting just the people on your team, either — connecting with people on other teams, in different roles and business areas, will pay dividends.

Mentorship and support

Nick: I received an amazing level of mentorship from several co-workers during my time at Palantir. My formal mentor took the time to meet with me multiple times a day to discuss progress, next steps, and to teach more more about Palantir’s data products and tech stack. At the same time, all of my teammates were willing to help me learn more about different components of the project throughout every day. It was incredible how invested every Palantirian was in the growth of the intern class.

Emily: I met with my mentor, who happened to be my team’s Tech Lead, at a minimum of once daily, though we often met twice a day via video conferencing with plenty of Slack communication throughout the day. She onboarded me to the team, helped determine suitable projects for me, and was my biggest thought partner and supporter in all things team. My lead, who supported exclusively USG interns, set up a few group events for us to hangout and get to know each other better in a casual/fun setting — we played Kahoot and Jackbox games together!

Ryan: No matter what you do, you’ll have mentorship from your mentor and lead. They’ll help you with ramping up, as well as technical and design questions. Aside from technical stuff, they’ll also help you with general internship tips and professional skills! My mentor helped set up lunches/hang outs with small groups of the team every week for the first month or so! It was a lot of fun being able to talk to some of the people I didn’t work closely with on a daily basis and learn about them. I was also included in all the planning/design meetings of current/future projects.

Impact

Emily: The impact of my work [on the COVID-19 response] was always clear, as Palantir’s COVID work was being discussed in the media so often. I could read news articles and know they were describing my team’s accomplishments, even if the article didn’t specifically mention Palantir. A few months following my internship, I read an article that highlighted and praised the quality of our data integration — an effort I contributed to during my internship.

Nick: I was really surprised (and excited) by the amount of work I was able to contribute to the team. Our team was working on four large initiatives for our client, and my mentor placed me in a leading role for two of them. Having worked in a couple of roles within the tech sphere, there was no other position where I was given this quantity of meaningful work. I was able to demo the products I worked on directly to our client, and I felt really valued in my team from day one.

Ryan: [The product I worked on] played a critical role in the success of the broader Foundry platform, where several product teams relied on his tech in order to properly function. In addition, service projects played a fundamental role in the big Foundry push for multitenancy! Because of this, I knew I wasn’t doing busy work and my projects played a central part in moving the company forward. People actually needed the stuff I was building, and that just pushed me to do it better.

Favorite virtual event

Nick: There were so many great experiences, but the king of all events has to be the virtual puzzlehunt. I’ve done puzzlehunts before with other organizations and companies, but the amount of effort and detail put into Palantir’s puzzlehunt was unmatched. We worked in teams over the course of a day, and everyone from the C-suite to the interns were involved. I’m really looking forward to seeing what it looks like this summer, and am looking to improve on last summer’s 17th place finish…

Ciara: Team game nights (you’d be surprised how fun virtual Pictionary is), and the PalNoir [PalNoir is Palantir’s internal affinity group for Palantirians that identify as Black or African-American] quarterly conferences that bring together the PalNoir community across all different parts of the world.

Emily: My favorite virtual event during Summer 2020 was the Puppy Battle [see below]. As a returning intern, one of the things I missed most about office culture was seeing all the dogs. Being able to vote for our favorites each day was exciting and different from our typical day-to-day.

Ryan: 100% the Dr. Karp AMA event. I thought it was incredibly cool that the CEO of a large tech company would take some time out of his day to personally meet a bunch of college kids. It was only an hour, but it was super fun talking with Dr. Karp, getting to know him, and hearing his thoughts about the company.

Conclusion

Working remotely may not be what you had in mind when you applied for an internship — but as our 2020 interns proved, with a little initiative and intentionality, you can make the most of your intern experience. We hope that whether you’re embarking on your first remote internship or a second summer of virtual meetings (and coding in your pajamas), these tips help you feel a little more prepared.
