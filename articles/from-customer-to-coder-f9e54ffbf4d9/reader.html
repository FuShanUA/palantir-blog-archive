UK Royal Navy officer reflects on Palantir fellowship experience

As part of Palantir’s ongoing mission to champion the UK Armed Forces and upskill personnel for the future of defence, Palantir is proud to participate in the Percy Hobart Fellowship. The 12-week Fellowship is run by PUBLIC on behalf of the UK Ministry of Defence and offers a training placement for Royal Navy and Royal Air Force officials, officers, and NCOs.

Fellows are paired with some of the most cutting-edge companies in science and technology and are offered mentoring, workshops, and course material to supplement their placements. The immersive, hands-on experience in innovation and technology equips fellows with lessons they can bring back to their Services to enhance innovation across defence.

In this post, Royal Navy Warrant Officer Class 2 (WO2) Rob Dye reflects on how his 12 weeks at Palantir offered him unparalleled insights into Palantir’s structure, workflows, and goals, taking him from customer to coder on a range of exciting projects.

UK Royal Navy Warrant Officer Rob Dye

When offered a 12-week placement with Palantir as part of the Royal Navy sponsored Percy Hobart innovation fellowship I couldn’t accept quickly enough.

I joined the Royal Marines 20 years ago, straight from school, and have become accustomed to the structure and culture that comes with military life. So, the chance to work inside an innovative tech organisation was like the gift I had always wanted but was too afraid to ask for.

Palantir wasn’t a new organisation to me. As the Technical Lead for the Royal Navy’s Project KRAKEN, which uses Foundry as the foundation for personnel management, amongst other workflows, I’ve been a customer of Palantir’s for the past six months. I’ve experienced first-hand how Palantir’s customer focused approach means they’ve been able to meet any challenges I’ve thrown at them.

But while the company was generally a known entity, I wasn’t expecting how welcomed I would be in the workplace. Being at Palantir gave me a chance to see just how the company’s culture, ethos, and continual feedback loops between business and product development teams truly enables those challenges to be met.

During my time, I’ve been fortunate enough to support and build solutions for several deployments across the organisation. From government to charities, Palantir supported my experience with assistance from the teams around me to quickly add value and meet the customer requirements.

My first experience building in Foundry saw me build an OSINT dashboard for an elephant conservation organisation. With advice from experienced Palantirians, I was able to ingest the raw data, clean and display visually for the customer in a choropleth map, meaning they could easily visualise incidents over time by region and begin integrating with additional data sources already in the platform. Now, that’s not to say my first attempt was perfect. But through direct customer feedback I was able to quickly amend the dashboards to suit their needs and deliver business value. Collaboration was the key to success.

Lessons learned: ‘If you can’t figure something out in 10 minutes, ask someone’

It is no secret that defence is deeply compartmentalised. Coming from this structure and stepping into this new environment took some adaption, both mentally and culturally. As I joined Palantir for onboarding, I was shocked with how willingly people reached out from across the whole organisation to say hello, discuss their experiences, and offer help or advice. I soon realised this was not a one-off experience, but was deeply rooted in Palantir’s culture. People are genuinely encouraged to reach out.

The best bit of advice I was given throughout my 12 weeks was that if you that if you cannot figure something out in under 10 minutes, ask someone, because there will almost definitely be someone around you who has experienced your problem before — and solved it. This isn’t to say everything always works, but there is a strong, well-practiced process in the company to ensure teams sit down and ask why something didn’t work and why something failed. I experienced this first hand when I joined a 5-whys session for a US forestry deployment focused on fire fighting, as the team established the root cause for why a cluster had been allowed to reach a critical point before resolution. The willingness across teams to interrogate mistakes, to make sure learnings are assessed and taken seriously, stops others from making the same mistakes. Doing the uncomfortable, often difficult, work of evaluating errors builds better teams and strengthens outputs for the long haul.

So, what’s next as I move back to defence and what are my big three learnings from Palantir?

  • Failure should be embraced; we only learn through understanding our failures, finding the root cause and putting processes in place to make sure they do not happen again.
  • Transparency through communication is key; without a transparent, open organisation you will lack trust and work will be conducted in isolation for fear of upsetting the applecart.
  • Adopting a flat structure doesn’t mean there isn’t a hierarchy or people will go rogue; rather, it means teams will have the space to grow and won’t be afraid to try new things because there is no such thing as a bad idea, just a failure to try.
