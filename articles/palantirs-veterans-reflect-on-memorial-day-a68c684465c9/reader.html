Palantir’s Veterans Reflect on Memorial Day

Palantir
7 min read

May 25, 2020

For many Americans, Memorial Day is synonymous with barbecuing in the backyard, visiting family, and ushering in summer. For those who have served in the military, this day holds a very different meaning. And while Memorial Day will look different this year, Palantir and the Pal-Vet Military Veteran community will pause to give the day its intended reverence.

This year we’ve decided to sponsor Carry the Load’s national Memorial May relay, in which volunteers cover more than 15,500 miles across the United States to spread awareness about those that sacrifice for our country. Carry the Load’s tagline, “Who are you carrying?”, aims to inspire Americans to remember, honor, and celebrate those individuals.

While many of the events of the relay have moved online due to the pandemic, we encourage you to learn how you can participate and show your support for Veterans, first responders, and their families. Now more than ever, it’s important for our nation to reflect on the sacrifices of those that keep us safe — whether military, first responder, or essential worker — and Memorial Day is a great place to start.

For many of us at Palantir, it’s personal. In this post, we’re sharing a few reflections from our Veteran community, as well as ways that you can honor those that have died in service to the United States. As our Veterans share who they carry this Memorial Day, we invite you to join us in reflection on the service and sacrifice of those lost, and to share their stories.

Dan R., United States Army

I grew up in suburban Chicago, so Memorial Day meant one thing to me: open pools. Sure, we had a parade and watched some older Vets walk down the street, but the pool was open and school was out — summer was in full swing. I guess it’s safe to say that growing up in a period of relative peacetime, and with no members of my family serving in the military outside of my Uncle during Vietnam and my Grandfather during WWII and Korea, Memorial Day just didn’t seem like something that was very relevant to me.

That perspective, of course, changed like so many things did on 9/11. I was a senior in high school, and we were a nation at War. Then — like now, during the coronavirus pandemic — the world became different seemingly overnight. Over the next 15 years, from my time at West Point to active duty and deployments, the real reason for Memorial Day became clear for me.

Our nation asks a very small percentage of people to volunteer to put their life on the line for our national values. The past 20 years and two wars remind us that not all of those people come home. For me, Memorial Day is deeply personal and brings with it a rollercoaster of emotions; sadness for those lost, guilt for surviving, anger at the situation and how the death might have been prevented, frustration by well intended but poorly timed “thank you for your service” greetings, worry about surviving families, and of course pride and excitement to share and honor the legacy of those lost.

CPT Andrew “PK” Pedersen-Keel was a classmate and a friend who was killed in action on March 11th 2013 in Afghanistan, and I often find my mind wandering to him on this day (and many others). I keep the prayer card from his funeral on the bookshelf in my office, and think of him often. On numerous occasions, my 5 year old son has asked me to “tell me about your Army friend.”

On this Memorial Day, I take pride in continuing to share PK’s legacy with my son. It’s a legacy of hard work, duty, and sacrifice. Who wouldn’t want their child to strive for those values?

Mike C., United States Army

From an early age, playing in the back yard with GI Joes, I knew I wanted to be part of the military and serve my country. With a truly unknown but unwavering desire, I was at my recruiting station on my 17th birthday with a parental waiver, and with my right hand raised and my chest out, I was determined to serve.

As a young adult, Memorial Day meant enjoying the sun and the water with friends, but as my time in the military progressed, the narrative changed for me. I learned what sacrifice meant and what so many gave to hold our country high. They were fathers, mothers, brothers, sisters, sons, daughters and so much more; they signed a blank check to serve and gave their pound of flesh willingly.

In the present, Memorial Day comes at a slower pace. My heart gets stretched as I think about all the friends and families who have endured ultimate sacrifice and I am proud to honor the ones who died defending America’s freedom. I’d imagine they all appreciate the sentiment, but that they’d also want everyone to enjoy the three-day weekend as much as they did. That just seems to be the kind of people they were: selfless, driven, and never shy to enjoy life at every opportunity. They will always have my deepest love and appreciation for their lasting sacrifice.

A good friend, platoon leader, and (at the time) team leader for 5th SFG, CPT Ben Tiffner, holds a special place in my heart. Every time I hear his name, his laugh comes with it. He was a legendary kind of guy, great leader and excellent in his craft. It’s people like Ben that keep me going and help give true meaning to this special holiday.

“All gave some, some gave all.”

Jaime M., United States Air Force

I’d like to say I joined the Air Force in a selfless act of service, but at the young age of 18, that was not the case. I joined because I thought it would be a challenge, it would offer a great education, and I thought I would get to travel and see the world. My career in the Air Force did in fact provide all of that, but I got so much more out of it than I ever imagined possible. And along the way, I came to understand what serving my country truly meant.

As a kid, and pre-Air Force life, Memorial Day meant a three-day weekend and the start of summer to me. Now, it’s a more somber day where I pay respects to those who have served and who did not come home, and to those who have served and have since passed. It is the one day of the year where our country is reminded that freedom is earned and defended at the expense of the lives of fathers and sons, mothers and daughters, brothers and sisters.

I’ll never forget where I was the day I received word that two of my classmates were killed in a training accident while serving in Iraq. Captain Derek Argel and Captain Jeremy Fresques were the first combat-related deaths of my class and they died exactly 4 years to the day that we all graduated from the US Air Force Academy together. I had just returned from a deployment myself and this news was a sobering reminder to me that not everyone returns. This is what it means to serve.

I hope that Americans enjoy this Memorial Day with family and friends, as well as many more Memorial Days to come. But I also hope that the true meaning of the “holiday” is not lost and that we continue to appreciate those that have devoted themselves and laid down their lives for this great nation. It is certainly something worth appreciating, and even more so, remembering.

Gabe S., United States Marine Corps

Growing up in a quiet suburb north of New York City, Memorial Day always meant one thing to me: spending the day with my paternal grandfather. The day began with a service down the street from my grandparent’s house at Myrtle Park, followed by marching in the parade down to Flint Park, where I’d eat hot dogs at the local American Legion where he was a Post Commander. Memorial Day was for the survivors to carry the load, to live well while never forgetting the sacrifice of the dead and the debt owed.

The Pax Americana that I grew up in changed like so many things did on 11 September, 2001. I was a sophomore in college, and lived with five friends in a residential tower at SUNY Albany. The three planes departing from Boston that day used our distinctive campus as a catching feature to signal the turn south into the Hudson Valley towards lower Manhattan. We watched live from our room as the second plane collided with the south tower. Four of my roommates would go on to serve in the global war on terror, and another would become an FDNY station chief; our war had begun, and it was time for us to put our chips down on the American experiment.

Memorial Day took on a new meaning for me in April 2012, when a Marine under my command, MSgt Scott Pruitt, was killed by a suicide bombing that wounded many others, myself included. But Memorial Day isn’t just about those who fall in enemy action. Midshipman 3/C Justin Zemser was part of our extended Naval Academy family during my wife’s tour teaching there, and was tragically killed when an Amtrak train derailed in Philadelphia on 12 May, 2015. Of all the loss my tribe has experienced over the past 19 years of war, Z is one of two faces I never have to summon when my mind wanders to their memory. He represented the best of America with his commitment to serving others and pursuing difficult goals while motivating all those around him.

Honoring the sacrifice of those lost during their time in service is about more than ceremonies, parades and barbecues, which won’t be taking place this year. On this Memorial Day, I’m renewing my commitment to sharing Z’s legacy of excellence to others, which is something we could all use more of in times like these.
