The Full Stack Engineering Experience at Palantir

When most people think of full stack engineering, they imagine developers who split their time between frontend and backend work. But at Palantir, full stack engineering means something entirely different. Our Full Stack Engineers don’t just write code on both sides of the application stack, they own entire product experiences, work directly with users to solve critical problems, and have the autonomy to make decisions that drive real impact.

What does it actually look like to be a Full Stack Engineer at a company building software that helps organizations make sense of their most complex data? We spoke with six Full Stack Engineers from across Palantir about their experiences. They shared what drew them to full stack work, the types of problems they tackle daily, and what makes engineering at Palantir unique.

Meet the Team

Our engineers span from recent hires to decade-long veterans, working across our global offices:

Joe (London, 2018) appreciates the ability to work on ambiguous problems. He notes, “You’re surrounded by abundant information and signal, without being directly told what to work on or how. You choose your work because you believe it’s the most effective way to improve the product.”

Philip (USA, 2017) emphasizes that the ability to “lean into your strengths, take ownership, and build great products” is very energizing and one of the main reasons he has enjoyed working at Palantir over the years.

Rory (USA, 2019) highlights the ownership she has over her work at Palantir, noting, “There’s plenty of space to voice your opinions on what we should build and why. While you don’t always win, it’s not a top-down approach.”

Seth (New York, 2023) values the high level of trust given to him, even as a new hire. “All engineers manage their products end-to-end and have significant flexibility and independence in making architectural decisions.”

Tim (Denver, 2013) is energized by “working on real problems today, turning them into products that thousands of users can use tomorrow”.

Will (New York, 2023) finds Palantir’s projects fast-paced and iterative. He values the close collaboration among engineers, designers, and product managers: “Everyone steps outside their comfort zones, takes responsibility for outcomes, and prioritizes the product’s end goal above all else.”

Here’s what they told us about building products that matter, from the database to the user interface and everything in between.

What do you love most about working as a Full Stack Engineer at Palantir?

Seth
I love the high rate of learning and growth that I’ve experienced at Palantir, particularly as a Full Stack Engineer. Because the breadth of knowledge needed is higher as a full stack dev, I’ve been exposed to so many different types of code, frameworks, and systems that I’ve constantly been learning over the past two years I’ve been working here, and I haven’t seen any signs of that slowing down.

Philip
A lot of people view full stack as a 50/50 split across frontend and backend, though in reality that’s rarely true. Our Full Stack Engineers epitomize the ‘go where you’re needed most’ mentality such that they work on the parts of the problem that are most important, or proximate, to achieving their team and project goals. A lot of times, we’ll have folks specialize in one facet, but know enough to be dangerous in the other. Or, that might change over time: you do a lot of frontend work on one project, and a lot of backend on the next. Full stack engineering at Palantir is all about being flexible and adaptable. We want people to lean into their spikes, but also be able to take high-level ownership of a problem space that’s irrespective of language or technology.

Joe
I really enjoy that I’m pushed from a product thinking and design perspective just as much as I am as an engineer. The engineering challenges I face are always in service of product goals that I fully believe in because I helped shape them. Above all, though, my favorite part of the job is the close contact with users I get. Going onsite to speak directly with our users at customer sites, and then being able to go back to my hotel and ship them a feature that they asked for, and then demo it to them the next day, is hugely exciting. It might be something as simple as adding a keyboard shortcut, or I might get signal from them that informs a multi-month project. Knowing I’m bringing value to those users and making software that makes them more effective is very motivating.

Tim
I enjoy working here as you get wide access to a variety of interesting problems. It’s the sweet spot between being a product company but also having real commercial and government customers with really important tasks. One minute I can be working on features for users optimizing workflows for hospitals, and then for users trying to understand supply chains for rockets, and then for users identifying tax fraud rings. The variety of problems is huge, we get to soak in the details and talk to the users directly, which is incredibly motivating. My goal is to enable individual users to solve their multi-million dollar problems.

Will
What I love most is the ability to ideate throughout the product and feature lifecycle, from determining the problem space and user needs, to technical modeling, to UX, and back through the cycle. I can add value to the product iteratively from the most user facing workflows back to our core technical decisions. This also means that I get to be in touch with all of our stakeholders from users and implementers to other product teams and help orchestrate work across all of these facets.

Rory
I have always enjoyed working closely with design, and as a frontend engineer, I get to actively contribute and collaborate with designers. One of the most rewarding aspects of the work is the immediate validation you get from seeing your changes reflected in the browser, along with the opportunity to collaborate closely with designers to shape how features and products should work.

What types of problems have you worked on as a Full Stack Engineer at Palantir?

Seth
As a Full Stack Engineer, I’m able to own features and changes end-to-end, from the database schema changes, to the logic and endpoint additions, to the UI changes in the frontend. This contributes to a greater feeling of ownership, as I’m not reliant on other devs to build the feature in a particular way that will work with my changes. For example, I built a feature that gives the ability for users to switch to a newer beta version of Foundry and Gotham. This allows users to opt-in to a newer version of these platforms to try out new features before they’re generally available.

Philip
We do a lot of work in the highside space: whether that be banking, domestic and foreign government, or other industries that require on-prem hardware. There’s a lot of internal infrastructure required for that work, and one of the more impactful full stack projects we’ve done is to rearchitect the alerting and triage systems used internally to identify, diagnose, and triage issues across all of our managed environments. Full stack engineering was particularly applicable here as it allowed a small, core group of engineers to work closely on solution design with our primary users and then swiftly deliver product iterations.

Joe
One of the things about our Foundry platform is that our workflows are often very information-dense and require a lot of data to back them. One theme of frontend engineering projects I’ve found really interesting at Palantir has been optimizing for intensive data-loading workflows such as these. Frameworks we’ve built or worked with have sought to balance ease of getting loaded data where it’s needed with batching, caching, and periodic reloading, while making the API for subscribing to and loading data easy to adopt.

Tim
Imagine you needed to build a cruise ship with thousands of cabins, engine rooms, mechanical departments. All with electrical, AC, and plumbing. It’s like a small city on the sea. You’d need access to all kinds of information about the individual components and the overall structure in order to plan something of this scale. You’d need to build a model of how long it would take for you to build each individual component, its supply chain, what needs to be done in what order, and how does it impact all of the other pieces. You need to be able to see through time how a delay to one component will delay other components. The data scale is huge, the 3D model is multiple gigabytes, the story is massive. Apply this to ships, planes, rockets, factories. Solving these kinds of problems is what a Full Stack Engineer enables. The need is today. It’s hard, there’s data integration, complex 3D visualization, data from different sources, but also doing that in a way that’s scalable across different problems.

Will
I’ve worked to create end-to-end applications that leverage Foundry-based data models to capture complex real world systems. These applications have involved event-driven services to perform complex aggregation logic and frontend interfaces to enable complex investigative workflows on derived data. One project was a full stack application to create and manage hierarchical structures and surface dynamic data layers on top of those hierarchies. This work is very important to customers for sustainment and logistics workflows in which many disparate source systems feed in updates to operations at a high scale.

Rory
Our work involves translating complex technical concepts into user-friendly interfaces that dynamically reflect intricate data relationships. To be an effective Frontend Engineer, you need to understand/interact/debug and sometimes build the layers above and below the layer that you work in and how those components stitch together. At Palantir, that means you may also build backend services that enable the user interface or work in Figma mocks to prototype an idea. Our ownership model empowers anyone to take initiative: if you notice something that isn’t working or want to see a change, you’re encouraged to own and drive those improvements. For example, when I saw an opportunity to improve a design, I created a mockup of my proposed solution and shared it with my teammates for feedback.

What makes being a Full Stack Engineer at Palantir different from other companies?

Seth
At Palantir, I’ve felt that the level of trust put into me even as a new hire was much higher than previous companies I’ve worked at. All engineers are entrusted to manage their products from end to end and given lots of flexibility and independence in making architectural decisions. This contributes to a faster paced environment as there isn’t red tape to go through to get your changes approved. The high level of ownership also contributes to growth in a significant way, and I feel that I’ve learned much more at Palantir in a much quicker time frame than anywhere else.

Philip
Palantir, as a whole, is known for giving its engineers an outsized amount of ownership from day one. There’s no ‘I must run this by X because X is in charge of Y.’ Instead, it’s a best idea wins company where its employees are enabled, and encouraged, to take as much ownership as they possibly can. Full stack engineering takes this a step further as you can own a whole product space irrespective of frontend and backend. Similarly, our engineers are known for working across the project lifecycle: scoping, planning, designing, and building. These roles might be filled by different people at another company, but the same or smaller set at Palantir.

Joe
Outcomes at Palantir are very much dictated by you and the team you work closely with. In my experience, you’re surrounded by an abundance of information and signal, and you’re not ever really directly told what it is that you should be working on or how you should go about it. This means that you do the work you’re doing because you chose it, you believe in it and think that it’s the most effective way to make the product better. There’s never a sense of doing something because that’s what you’ve been told you’re working on.

Tim
It’s the people. We’re a global company with people from all different walks of life, backgrounds and specialties, but still a smaller company with an elite squad of people. Our hiring bar is very high for a reason. You’re exclusively working around highly competent people with heavily differentiated skillsets. There’s deep integration between Product Development teams and Business Development teams from many different industries, so as a Full Stack Engineer you have access to all that knowledge and the diverse set of skills those people bring.

Will
Palantir’s mission influences how I approach problems by putting impact on our users and their mission above all else. It keeps me and everyone around me grounded in the shared understanding that the decisions and tradeoffs we make about how we allocate our resources all resolve back to how we drive impact for others. I have 100% ownership over the features that I build and am responsible both for the successes and failures of my ideas.

Rory
At Palantir, the product is less domain-specific and more tool-oriented, so our job is to build flexible solutions that others can adapt to their own domains while also having some understanding of what those domains are (i.e., I build tooling for people who want to visualize data on maps, and while I need to understand the map, I also need to ensure that it integrates with a generic ontology and accommodates workflows across many domains like utilities and supply chain tracking). As an engineer, I rarely just receive designs to implement; instead, the process is highly iterative and collaborative. PMs, designers, and engineers work together from ideation through delivery — discussing what to build, why it matters, and how to measure success. This means engineers are involved in every stage, from design to delivery.

What advice would you give to someone aspiring to be a Full Stack Engineer at Palantir?

Seth
Given that full stack engineering is more flexible, I think it’s even more important for aspiring Full Stack Engineers to learn how to think and problem-solve generally, as opposed to learning any specific tool or framework. If you’re great at problem solving, you’ll still be effective no matter what the current most popular framework is.

Philip
Lean into the 80/20 principle! The best Full Stack Engineers I know are the ones who know enough to be dangerous about most things, but stick to their most polished skills the majority of the time. Just because you can build something does not mean it’s the most efficient means of work. Unblocking yourself is a key skill of a Full Stack Engineer, but it doesn’t mean you must do everything. Especially at the start, when you’re building lots of new skills, don’t be afraid to ask for help when needed or when you might be in over your head.

Joe
Think about product almost as much as you think about engineering. Skills like talking to users, whiteboarding, and writing clean, readable code all come together in a day’s work for a Full Stack Engineer at Palantir.

Tim
Problem decomposition is vital. A big part of deciding how to build something is deciding what to build. This requires properly decomposing a user’s larger problem or workflow into its fundamental pieces. This lets us figure out what we can deliver tomorrow that can provide value in the short term, and that we can build on top of next week to compound that value. Solve the problem as soon as possible, but then solve it better over time. This is fundamental to both product building and technical planning: reducing the problem to its smallest elements, deciding what order to build them in, and how you’re going to get feedback on it. You can practice this — take your favorite website or app; how would you build that? How can you build a simple version this weekend that you can continue to build up over time, in a way that can scale from a prototype to production?

Will
I would advise someone aspiring to be a Full Stack Engineer at Palantir to keep in mind that being full stack is much more than just the combination of backend and frontend. More than that combination, it means being a product ideator, a technical contributor, a user empath, a product manager, and much more. Full stack embodies what every engineer at Palantir already does, which is to take ownership of new challenges no matter how they fit into their existing vision of their role.

Rory
Figure out what makes you want to do this work. Do you care about tooling? User experience? Deep technical complexity? Hone in on the thing that drives you, and there is surely a niche within Palantir (frontend, backend, infrastructure, or otherwise) that fits your spikes. Do you care more about the elegance of language and architecture? Or do you care deeply about how people use that tool? Figuring out what drives you will help you build a lot of purpose in your work.
