In this blog post, Nikita, a Frontend engineer based in DC, shares how rendering accurate circles on maps led him through map projections, spherical trigonometry, and the surprising complexity of visualizing ranges in polar regions.

A few months ago, a customer asked us to visualize the strike range of naval vessels operating in polar regions. Simple enough, right? Draw some circles on a map. Except when we tried the obvious approach, the circles looked like ellipses, cut off at weird angles, and sometimes wrapped around the world entirely. What should have been a simple feature turned into one of the more interesting frontend problems I’ve worked on at Palantir as a Frontend Engineer.

Why Circles Matter

Before we dive into the technical challenge, it’s worth understanding why this matters. At Palantir, we build software for people working on critical defense and intelligence problems with Palantir Gotham, our platform for integrating, analyzing and operationalizing the complex data their missions depend on. When these teams visualize geospatial data, accuracy is essential.

A circle on a map might represent:

  • The range of a missile defense system.
  • The coverage area of a cell tower network.
  • The blast radius of an explosive threat.
  • The operational range of a naval vessel.

If these visualizations are wrong, decisions get made with incorrect information. That’s not acceptable.

Map Visuals

In many development contexts, we leverage MapLibre, an open-source mapping library, to visualize geospatial data. To draw shapes — lines, polygons, points — we feed MapLibre geometries in GeoJSON format.

GeoJSON is perfect for web applications: it’s just JSON, so it works natively in JavaScript without conversion. It’s human-readable, lightweight, and the de facto standard for web mapping. MapLibre’s APIs are built around it. But the catch is, GeoJSON spec doesn’t include circles. The spec supports LineStrings, Polygons, Points, and various collections of these — but no circles.

This isn’t an oversight. Unlike polygons, which are defined purely by their coordinate vertices, circles require a radius — and that means choosing a unit. Should the radius be in meters? Miles? Degrees of latitude? Each choice leads to different results, and GeoJSON deliberately avoids encoding these kinds of measurement decisions into its geometry types. The spec sticks to coordinate-based shapes that work universally, leaving distance calculations and units to the application layer.

But there’s a deeper problem with circles on web maps, one that only becomes apparent when you try to implement them: map projections fundamentally distort what a “circle” even means.

Let’s Try Implementing

The naive solution is obvious: a circle is just a polygon with lots of points. Sample points around the circumference, connect them, and you’ve got yourself a circle.

Perfect! Well, almost — a sharp eye can see the individual sampled points and the straight lines connecting them. We can increase the sample rate to combat that. But for most purposes, this works great. Now let’s try the same technique with a radius of a few thousand miles.

Wait, what happened? The resulting shape looks like an ellipse on the flat map. But when we render the same points on a 3D globe, they form a perfect circle. This is where things get interesting. It is time we talk about map projections.

Map Projections

In cartography, a map projection attempts to represent the curved surface of a globe on a two-dimensional plane of a map. Here’s the fundamental issue: you cannot flatten a sphere onto a plane without distortion. All projections of a sphere on a plane necessarily distort the surface in some way. Every map you’ve ever seen is a compromise. All projections distort something — area, shape, distance, or direction.

Different projections make different trade-offs: Lambert Conformal Conic preserves angles for aeronautical charts but distorts areas; Albers Equal Area preserves area for thematic maps but warps shapes; Robinson splits the difference aesthetically but sacrifices accuracy entirely.

The most common projection is the Mercator projection. It’s the projection used by Google Maps, most GPS systems and many classroom maps. The Mercator projection became the dominant world map after its creation in 1569, because it’s exceptionally useful for navigation — any straight line on the map represents a constant compass bearing, making it simple for sailors to plot and follow courses. This practical advantage meant it remained the standard for centuries. As with other projections, the Mercator projection also distorts the true layout of Earth’s surface. It exaggerates areas far from the equator; the closer to Earth’s poles, the greater the distortion. One common example is that Greenland and Africa appear to be the same size, when in reality Africa is actually 14 times the size of Greenland.

There’s also an online tool that lets you see the real sizes of different countries: https://thetruesize.com/

Today, most online mapping services (including MapLibre) use Web Mercator, a modern variant optimized for digital maps. It’s computationally efficient and preserves local shapes well, which is why your neighborhood looks correct on Google Maps. But it still massively distorts areas near the poles. This is exactly the reason why our circle looks like an ellipse. At large scales, the projection’s distortion warps the visualization. The circle is correct on the sphere of Earth, but looks distorted on the flat map we’re rendering.

Here’s what’s happening: the leftmost and rightmost points of our sampled circle shouldn’t be connected directly, rather they need to wrap around the world and connect with the copies of the circle on the copies of the map. Exploring the Implementation

The real implementation is more complex than the high-level solution I described above. Let’s break it down into the key pieces: sampling points around the circumference, creating the circle outline and generating the colored fill.

Sampling Points Around the Circumference

First, we need a way to find a point given a center of a circle, radius, and bearing. This involves some spherical trigonometry (using haversine formula) that I will spare you the details of, but you can use open-source solutions like Turf.js’s destination function:

Now, getting points around the circumference is straightforward: split 360 degrees into numPoints equal angles, and calculate the destination point at each bearing:

function getSampledCirclePoints(center, radius, numPoints) {
  const coordinates = [];
  
  for (let i = 0; i < numPoints; i++) {
    // It is -360 because we go in counterclockwise order
    // as per geojson polygon spec!
    const bearing = (i * -360) / numPoints;
    coordinates.push(
      destination(center, radius, bearing)
    );
  }
  
  return coordinates;
}

Handling the Circle Outline

Remember how circles crossing the poles aren’t closed polygons, but rather sinusoidal lines? This means we need to use GeoJSON’s LineString type instead of Polygon for the outline. The tricky part is finding the two coordinates that cut off the circle. This is when the line should cross the antimeridian (the International Date Line at 180 degrees longitude, which divides the planet into Eastern and Western hemispheres) instead. Here's the core logic:

function createPolarAwareCircleOutline(sampledPoints) {
  const lineCoordinates = [sampledPoints[0]];
  
  for (let i = 1; i < sampledPoints.length; i++) {
    const point = sampledPoints[i];
    const prevPoint = sampledPoints[i - 1];
    
    if (shouldCrossAntimeridian(point.lon, prevPoint.lon)) {
      // Adjust longitude to render on the next world copy
      const adjustedLon = point.lon + (point.lon < 0 ? 360 : -360);
      lineCoordinates.push([adjustedLon, point.lat]);
    } else {
      lineCoordinates.push([point.lon, point.lat]);
    }
  }
  
  return {
    type: "LineString",
    coordinates: lineCoordinates
  };
}
// If 2 points are more than half the world apart, we should cross the antimeridian!
function shouldCrossAntimeridian(lon1, lon2) {
  return Math.abs(lon1 - lon2) > 180;
}

The clever trick here is that MapLibre allows longitudes beyond the standard -180 to 180 degrees range. For example, a longitude of 200 degrees tells MapLibre to render the point on the next world copy to the east. This is how we make the circle wrap around correctly.

Handling the Colored Fill

The colored fill is trickier because it must be a closed polygon. If the circle includes a pole, we need to explicitly extend the polygon to that pole to ensure the fill covers the entire area. What made this project particularly rewarding wasn’t just solving an interesting geometry problem — it was understanding the real-world stakes behind it. Our customers need accuracy at both the meter and the thousand-mile scale. They need it to work at the equator and around the poles. Understanding these requirements pushed me to solve the complete version of this problem, not just the simple “draw a circle on a map” version.
