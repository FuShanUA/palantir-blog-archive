Tariffs Are the New Norm

In recent decades, the global trading system has been centered on multilateral agreements and relatively open market access. This model allowed businesses to source low-cost goods from a variety of regions with minimal restrictions, encouraging industries to build intricate, interdependent supply chains and design operations around just-in-time practices.

Yet, as tariffs rise globally, this system faces rapid and cascading strain. Companies that once relied on a steady flow of finished or unfinished goods from a single country may suddenly encounter high tariffs, which reverberate through every aspect of their operations:

  • Increased Import Costs: Tariffs increase the cost of importing goods, leading to compounded effects on pricing strategies, profit margins, and overall competitiveness. Costs can spike not only when goods cross borders but also when countries implement retaliatory measures, causing immediate price volatility.
  • Supplier Replacement Challenges: Enterprises seeking to replace existing suppliers may be unaware of the terms in their existing contracts, risking litigation or hefty penalties for early termination if they attempt to switch to partners.
  • Sourcing and Onboarding New Suppliers: Even if companies overcome potential legal hurdles, they must identify new suppliers. Which regions offer the right capacity, quality, and pricing? How quickly can they source and onboard a new partner without disrupting production or compromising quality standards?
  • Managing Additional Costs: Ultimately, businesses will likely face unavoidable added costs. How much of these costs should be passed on to consumers? Can they find efficiencies internally that could absorb those costs without affecting consumers? Should they shelve certain products to maintain profitability?

Companies cannot afford to dismiss these challenges as mere political anomalies. While each US presidential administration may impose tariffs with varying levels and targets, support for these measures now transcends party lines. The American economy must reorient itself for a period of controlled market access and higher import costs. Doing so will require a fundamental shift in how businesses source supplies, manage inventories, and price their products. Although such changes typically unfold over the course of months or years, enterprises do not have the luxury of time to adapt.
