Internship Reflections: Working in Palantir’s D.C. hub

2022-03-10 · Original →

The Interns Go to Washington

In the summer of 2021, Palantir’s internship program operated in a hybrid model. Unlike in previous years, where interns were located across many office locations, interns had the option of either working remotely or working in-person from our London or Washington, D.C. offices.

We invited interns who worked in the Washington, D.C. office to share their perspective on finding an in-person community at Palantir and talk about their experience in one of our busiest offices.

What made your internship experience exciting?
Miguel: Coming from an academic background — I did lab research, theoretical work, and research writing alongside my undergraduate courses — I was very new to the world of software engineering. Thanks to the help of everyone on my team, I was able to learn about software engineering, and not just from a technical perspective. My mentor handled technical questions, and my lead connected me with people across the company, so I learned more about different culture and growth opportunities. After the internship, I feel more attuned to how a company like Palantir works and how my code connects with the bigger picture.

Saaniya: I definitely believe my confidence grew a lot during this internship, mainly due to the support I was given by my mentor and team lead to undertake projects that were out of my comfort zone but that they believed were well within my capabilities. I think just having those people in my corner — people who provided feedback on how to grow into the best version of yourself — gave me the comfort to truly explore uncharted technical territory.

Michael: Not only was I able to learn new programming languages, cloud technologies, and tools, I was able to rapidly improve my debugging and code quality processes. This helped me to establish my own independence in a large codebase and drove my confidence and initiative to think beyond the scope of my project or explore my own curiosities. I was encouraged to present my newfound experience and ideas to many others in the company and became much more comfortable spreading my expertise to educate and help others.

What was your support system like during the internship?
Michael: My core intern team was comprised of a mentor, lead, and ally, but the mentorship I received expanded to my team, other interns, and everyone I reached out to for a 1:1 chat. My mentor and I collaborated every day on technical problems, explored D.C. during our outdoor 1:1 walks, and even did some rock climbing at the same gym. Fellow interns cheered for each others’ success and everyone I worked with tried their hardest to enable me to achieve the best result by the end of the summer. I really felt that everyone wanted the best outcome for everyone else.

Saaniya: My support system during the internship was amazing and one of the big reasons why I am so excited to return in the fall. My mentor, team lead, and lead of the group I was a part of made sure to provide me with constructive feedback every week, which led me to both become a better engineer and also gain confidence in my abilities.

What advice would you give someone considering an internship at Palantir?
Miguel: Meet new people, as often as possible. There are some structured ways of doing this, including attending lunch talks and signing up for an (optional) “Random Sync,” which automatically matches you with another Palantirian. Also, be creative about how you meet new people. Ask mentors and leads to connect you with people you can learn from. Approach people at lunch or dinner, if it makes sense to do so. Hang out with the other interns, during and after work. The people at Palantir are so friendly and willing to impart their knowledge and experiences.

Saaniya: I would tell someone who is considering an internship at Palantir that if they love the scrappy feeling of a startup while also having the support structure of a company, Palantir is the place for them. The Palantir mentality of “if something is broken, fix it, no matter your role” is something that I’ve found to be unique to Palantir, having worked at a couple of other internships

Michael: The Palantir process was one of the most challenging I have ever completed, but it challenged all the right skills in the best way possible. The claims that Palantir makes around curating internship experiences that involve meaningful work could not be more true and easily outperform any company I’ve been a part of previously. They invest a lot of time and energy into understanding your skills and interests and match you with a problem/project and team that will flourish because of your presence.

Washington, D.C. is also the cultural heart of our Gotham and United States Government work. Our work with the U.S. Government and Defense sector is not only how we started, but also a large part of the current landscape of Palantir. Here in Washington, D.C., our interns working on U.S. Government and Defense had a multitude of opportunities including close proximity to clients, ability to see the immediate impact of their work, and addressing some of the nation’s most important challenges.

What was your favorite aspect of interning in Washington, D.C.?
Miguel: The office is located in the Georgetown neighborhood, an incredibly vibrant place. During the summer, I visited the movie theater, the waterfront park, many restaurants featuring all sorts of cuisines, and the flea market on Sunday mornings. I highly recommend taking the weeknights and weekends to explore Georgetown, or any other part of D.C. That’s how I was able to decide that D.C. will be my new home after graduation!

Saaniya: My favorite part of interning in Washington, D.C. was being able to experience a new city as a working adult. The last time I had been to DC was during my eighth grade school field trip, and being able to come back seven years later and immerse myself in the same culture and environment, but as a resident, was a very cool experience.

Michael: All summer, the weather in D.C. was sunny and made walks to the office all the more enjoyable. The housing that Palantir provided proved to not only be the most fun location for ping pong, pool, swimming, karaoke, and movies but was also within walking distance of D.C.’s signature landmarks and museums. Since I worked on problems related to Palantir’s U.S. Government clientele, the D.C. area was that much cooler to me, as I was able to cross paths with so many others thinking about the same problems I was. I left D.C. with a richer appreciation of the history of the area as well as a strong affinity toward the important and necessary work being done to improve national security.

What part of your work or internship made you the most proud?
Miguel: Toward the end of my internship, I worked on a small feature with the guidance of my mentor. As it turned out, this feature became a significant point of discussion, even after I had finished building version 1.0 of it. In short, the presence of this feature inspired a larger conversation about the high-level architecture of our code, which resulted in long-term plans to change the architecture. My experience with this feature is proof that any intern at Palantir can do work that is not only productive, but also transformative beyond the code itself.

Saaniya: The part of my internship that made me the most proud was taking a step back at the end of the internship and seeing just how much I grew in confidence and technical ability. It was amazing to me that by week eight of my internship, I was on calls with senior engineers at Palantir helping them debug their issues. I think this is a testament to just how much trust is given to interns because they’re all very capable of the work they’ve been given.

Michael: Only a few weeks after I had completed my internship, it was announced that Palantir was selected for the Army’s Capability Drop 2 (CD-2) program to deliver the Army’s intelligence data fabric and analytics foundation. CD-2 had been a program and effort that I had heard about internally since the first week of my internship and was a big driver in the project I contributed to all summer. Both the importance of CD-2 to Palantir and the Army and the technicalities of Palantir’s deliverables were new problem spaces that I had never worked in before, but being able to work on these problems and see the contract awarded to Palantir was something I remain very proud of.
