Stopping Modern Financial Crimes Requires a Modern Approach

2022-05-10 · Original →

Today’s financial criminal is sophisticated; so, too, must be today’s anti-money laundering strategies

Editor’s Note: On 10 May 2022, Palantir’s Daniel Nagle (Head of Financial Crimes Solutions) sat down with Mae Cheng of the Barron’s Group at the Wall Street Journal Risk & Compliance Forum to discuss the role of technology in Anti-Financial Crimes. This blog post is an expanded excerpt of the session.

Why are many financial institutions today vulnerable to financial crimes?

Anti-financial crime teams often find themselves in a challenging work environment. While their decisions are often critical in ultimately stopping financial crime, they often operate with poor data, legacy or fragmented solutions, and incomplete information. Even as the modern criminal has become more agile and technically savvy, many financial institutions have not been operating and implementing new technology at the same pace, putting compliance officers at an extreme disadvantage.

In an era of highly publicized anti-financial crime failures and swift regulatory actions, compliance teams must navigate between the fear of failure and the need to innovate. They must test new technology and processes to fight the future of financial crime. This begs the question: how do we modernize the data ecosystem, and how can we bolster our countermeasures? Giving people the right tools and facilities to action intelligence, rather than decompose different data elements into something that makes sense, will unlock a new era of financial crime compliance.

Combining human intellect and subject matter expertise with targeted, operations-focused artificial intelligence and data analysis can help organizations create a proactive and innovative compliance culture.

What does the modern criminal look like in the context of financial crime?

Criminals have evolved. The modern criminal is a data science-driven, sophisticated, strategic entity who focuses on investigating specific target institutions before striking. They are agile and data-centric. They understand the nuances between social engineering and data engineering, and they use both to effect their desired outcomes. The modern criminal understands the intricacies of business processes surrounding financial crime detection and is sharply focused on the gaps created by ineffective and inefficient systems.

There is no silver bullet to stop the rise of the modern criminal, but augmenting human intelligence with modern technology and a proactive compliance program can give us a chance.

Why are legacy technologies inadequate to stopping financial crime?

The financial sector deals with a myriad of different technology issues on a day-to-day basis. Financial institutions have to contend with point technology solutions that are expensive, inflexible, and difficult to use. Many data modernization projects haven’t delivered on the purported value they claimed they’d provide, and the industry still has issues with data quality, data availability, and the politicization of data through fiefdoms and silos.

Legacy technology is focused on single-issue counter measures such as a basic network analysis, a decision tree, and a model that sits for an extended lifetime. Situations like these are a paradise that the modern criminal searches for.

The modern criminal actively seeks out institutions that are not focused on modernization, data science, and the power of machine learning and artificial intelligence. Given the lack of maturity across the sector in these areas and the lack of availability of key resources at financial institutions, it is becoming increasingly difficult to drive the rigor and the development necessary in order to detect, report, and eventually stop the modern criminal.

Financial institutions should have the satisfaction in knowing that their regulatory dealings are improving their jurisdictions ability to target criminal elements and protect both retail and institutional clients.

What needs to change? Where is the innovation happening?

Today’s money laundering problems require a modern solution.

Some institutions may believe that shiny visualizations mean they are beating criminal organizations. Yes, they look pretty, but this doesn’t infer the reality. This is especially true when you consider the antiquated rules, segments, and models that these visualizations sit on that are well past their “best before” date.

Understanding the intricacies of an entity, resolving identity, and putting a maniacal focus on accuracy are some of the fundamental building blocks that we’ve built Palantir Foundry’s anti-money laundering capabilities around. We have become zealous in our ambitions to bring critical infrastructure for targeted, operationally relevant artificial intelligence and machine learning tools to the core problems that financial institutions are focused on.

We believe in the tantamount importance of solving for money laundering and financial crimes now. But the solution here is not a singular focus on just technology. In actuality, the focus for us is on the complexities of improving productivity, operationalizing intelligence, and lifting analysts from the mundane Sisyphean efforts of dealing with constant backlogs, false positives, and bad alerts. To achieve this, you need a layered analytical approach that combines mechanisms to simplify the complex, amplify the productive, and enable human beings to do what they are best at.

Our challenge to financial institutions and their anti-financial crime teams is to reflect on their successes and failures and start a conversation on how to improve the effectiveness of financial crime prevention. The modern criminal is not waiting.
