Building the Digital Grid: The role of software in grid resilience

2021-05-13 · Original →

A challenging decade for utilities

Over the last ten years, changes in the power sector have challenged the stability and safety of the electrical grid. A few major changes include:

  • The shifting mix of production technologies. A fall in the cost of renewables, pressure to meet the climate crisis, and political and investor decisions about coal and nuclear are all impacting grid stability and the cost of management.
  • Changing patterns of demand. Trends including electric vehicle ownership, distributed generation, and smart meter enabled services (e.g., time-of-day pricing) are introducing new patterns of demand.
  • An unpredictable climate. Volatile events like floods and hurricanes damage infrastructure, while hot and irregular weather increases the risk of grid-related wildfires and reduces the predictability of demand.

These challenges are compounded by aging grid infrastructure that is becoming more and more expensive to maintain. Many of them also have a political aspect. As governments create policies to mitigate the physical risks of climate change, for example, or accelerate the rollout of renewable generation, they reveal pressures and costs in the grid. Grid operators urgently need new infrastructure, better maintenance regimes, and more efficient systems to satisfy customers and regulators and guarantee the resilience of the grid.

Data-driven maintenance

To maintain the grid with the resources they have, grid operators need to know which assets to replace, and schedule the work within the right timeframe. This should be informed by a complete picture of risk and a framework for prioritization decisions that ensures a good return on each new component.

Grid operators have access to vast volumes of information about their assets. However, many lack the digital infrastructure to apply that information to complex prioritization decisions. Often operators are only able to access asset information in static reports that lack context and make it difficult to compare across asset types. As a result, rather than being driven by the data, decisions are made using heuristics like age or “first break first fix”.

To meet the challenges facing the grid, operators need to move beyond static analysis and siloed information. They need a complete view of their data and a risk scoring system that links directly to investment prioritization decisions. This, in turn, should drive maintenance operations, with risk scores provided to decision-makers alongside relevant information from other systems, like finance and organizational constraints.

Developing the digital grid

Connecting data to grid operations is not easy. To deliver power safely to customers, operators have to track the real time status of all outages. They need to know the location of every element of their grid and the characteristics of each region — corrosion and snow loading zones for example. They must track all past inspections and repairs, know their customers, map vegetation around lines, predict the weather, and understand the status and location of planned work. On top of all of this, they have streams of sensor data flowing in that quickly reach massive scale and require advanced technology to process.

Bringing these sources of information together to make informed decisions is a major challenge. It is often tackled via time-intensive manual processes. An engineer investigating asset failures, for example, might load up the relevant outages in one system, access information on past inspections in another system, and check against planned repairs in a third. Yet another system might hold information about budget and finances, making it hard to understand the cost of each intervention and the best way to allocate funds. All of this work to gather information only applies to a single point in time, so the result is quickly out of date.

We’ve deployed Foundry, Palantir’s enterprise data platform, to provide a digital grid that helps cut back these time-intensive workflows. Foundry enables information from every part of the grid to be integrated securely in a central data operating system. This provides a picture of real-time grid state estimation, accelerating workflows like the one characterized above and unlocking uses of data that were previously too technically challenging.

One partner, for example, was able to make use of an earlier investment in smart meters to improve customer service. Using Foundry, the company could integrate high frequency data streams from thousands of smart meters that contained information about the relationship between meters. This allowed them to algorithmically identify errors in the mapping between grid assets and customers, and to remedy mis-mapping where it might have led to customers being cut off from power without warning.

Another workflow unlocked by Foundry is the use of smart meter data, paired with other sensors, to identify patterns that signal a high risk of asset failures. When such patterns are identified, alerts are surfaced to analysts who can evaluate them — again making use of multiple sources of information — and dispatch preventative maintenance crews as required.

These are advanced use cases, but they illustrate just how quickly innovative approaches to maintenance can be developed once grid operators have centralized access to their data.

Risk-based maintenance: the gold standard

Of course, technology is only one part of the solution. Investment decisions are a dynamic, human driven process and there are constraints that data won’t capture — political pressures, for example, and the dynamic market in which utilities operate.

What Foundry has been able to offer partners in the utilities sector is a platform for teams to collaborate with all the relevant information. It’s not a magic bullet, but it allows decision-makers to work together with as much information as possible and link decisions to the evidence that informed them. As an example, in Foundry, the teams that collect data on assets and the teams that make maintenance decisions are able to work together. Team A can develop a risk model using all the relevant data about asset health, and Team B can use that model, combined with finance information, to prioritise investments.

On the basis of this experience, we’ve formulated a gold standard for digital grid software with four key requirements:

  • Integrate siloed systems. Bring all data and analytical assets into a centralized platform. Link data, analysis, and models together to unlock complex analytics like multi-model risk scoring. Ensure there is flexibility to integrate new information as things change.
  • Offer cross-organizational collaboration and accountability. Allow teams to work together in one environment and configure access controls to enable collaboration in line with data governance requirements. Capture decisions in processes that are needed to align priorities: long term resilience doesn’t just mean understanding what work to prioritize. It involves breaking down team silos that incentivize local, rather than global optimizations.
  • Establish a flexible framework for asset risk. Use integrated data and analytics to compile multiple measures of risk, for example by combining asset attributes, smart meter sensors and monitoring, outage analytics, failure and load data. Identify gaps in the data and put measures in place to remedy them — incomplete information gathering is one of the biggest barriers we encounter to data driven risk assessment. Set up models for flexibility in the future as new risk factors, such as greater EV use, evolve.
  • Enable opportunity simulation. Priorities for grid operation will continue to change. Software needs to enable data and models to be combined to simulate the possible tradeoffs of different long term choices.

When these requirements are met, operators can make informed decisions about investment based on all of the available information and the simulated outcomes of different decisions. By investing in gold standard software to drive spend on physical infrastructure, grid operators can make decisions that continuously bolster resilience and increase safety. In turn, by feeding these decisions back into their data, they build a digital asset that develops in tandem with the physical grid, informing a flexible and robust approach to maintenance for many years to come.
