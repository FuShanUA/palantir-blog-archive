Break the Machine: Hack Week 2021

2021-10-15 · Original →

Since our earliest days as a company, Hack Week has been a hallowed tradition at Palantir. It’s a time to set aside day-to-day work to explore those novel and even heretical ideas that have the potential to totally change Palantir — and the world. As our COO Shyam Sankar said in our Q2 Earnings Call, many of our best and most profound ideas originated from past Hack Weeks (including the precursor to Apollo, Palantir’s continuous delivery software for SaaS platforms). But this year’s event posed novel challenges. In this post, we discuss how Palantir adapted Hack Week to meet the moment in 2021.

The big ideas aren’t always on the roadmap. The big ideas come out of inspiration and perspiration. And I am keenly aware as management that the very best ideas — the most profound ideas — they start as heresy. — Shyam Sankar, Palantir COO

New Year, New Challenges

Over the past year, our teams have been working around the clock to help governments and organizations respond to the COVID-19 crisis. We’re so proud of the work we did (and are still doing!) to help our partners manage the pandemic. But the pace of the last 18 months also meant it was hard to create space for personal investigation and exploration. We also had over 500 new Palantirians start during that time, so we needed to find ways of engaging those people who might never have even met another Palantirian in person.

With all this in mind, we knew we had to rethink the way we’d structured Hack Weeks in the past. In prior years, we’ve focused a lot on the competitive aspect, but that tended to incentivize flashier projects and disincentivize the kind of high-risk, high-reward projects that have made past Hack Weeks so valuable. This year, we wanted to instead incentivize widespread participation and get back to the real, almost heretical spirit of Hack Week. This spirit was encapsulated in our theme, which was “Break the Machine.”

Building the Machine

One thing we did was invest in tools and structures to make the event successful. That included socializing potential project ideas on a leaderboard hosted on Palantir servers. Any Palantirian could add their own ideas to recruit fellow hackers, or see existing ideas and join those groups. In addition to getting people excited to hack, this had the added benefit of fostering teams that cut across job functions and parts of the business — which is something that’s generally been harder to facilitate in a world where not everyone is coming into offices. To foster team-building, we also provided funds so that Hack Week participants could gather, eat, drink, and build together. Ultimately, these efforts resulted in the highest participation of any Hack Week in Palantir history, with almost 900 Palantirians (out of 2700 employees) hacking and over 100 projects submitted. (Other projects, like self-directed learning or paper drafting, also took place behind the scenes and so weren’t captured in these submission totals.)

While we focused a lot on participation, it was also important that the competition aspect of Hack Week remained fair and unbiased. This year, a Palantir intern built a robust voting portal using our Foundry platform. The voting portal ensured that every project was viewed by the same number of voters across Palantir, so that projects built by the newest employee (or intern!) were given the same chance to impress as those from our most tenured employees.

Projects

Of the more than 100 projects submitted, 12 advanced to the finals. The final judges selected three winners:

  • Hack Fast BAS: This project helped rebuild the broad area search (BAS) imagery pipeline within Palantir Gotham, specifically focused on high-scale image processing and realtime reprioritization of a GPU cluster.
  • Foundry Containers: For implementations of Palantir Foundry in the life sciences, this project enhanced the ability to securely run open-source software like Jupyter Notebook, ImageJ, and more.
  • Garble: Foundry Data in Gaia: This project allows users to apply the full suite of Gaia’s geo-analysis tools against Foundry data, giving users the full power of Gaia and Foundry at their fingertips.

What Didn’t Work

While Hack Week 2021 was definitely a success, there are also plenty of things we didn’t get quite right — and that, in the spirit of breaking the machine, we plan on improving in future years. Those include:

  • Team-building: While our project portal made it easy for people to submit and view projects, it wasn’t as useful for actually building out teams. We need to figure out a more intuitive, easy-to-use way for people to form and join teams — especially people who are newer to the company and don’t have existing networks. On the other end of the spectrum, some people dabbled in multiple projects but ultimately didn’t commit to any of them. We need to figure out the last-mile problem of getting dabblers to commit to a single project — because otherwise they often wind up dropping out of the event entirely.
  • Voting: In previous years, we’ve separated the votes by category, awarding the highest-voted projects per category. The problem with that approach is that sometimes second-place projects in more competitive categories wind up feeling snubbed. So this year, we decided to do away with category-based voting and go with a “direct democracy” approach, advancing the highest-voted projects to finals, regardless of category. What we didn’t foresee was that this would wind up disadvantaging projects from less-populous parts of our business, which had smaller constituencies. Moving forward, we’re thinking about different voting schemes to try and more accurately reflect our goals, including quadratic voting, representative democracy, participation-based voting tokens, and a more complex multi-tiered “graph-based” system — we’ll continue experimenting and report back!
  • Cross-pollination: One of the benefits of Hack Week, at least in theory, is that it’s an opportunity for people from different parts of the business to work together. However, we realized that there was an asymmetry in who knew people from other parts of the business well enough to feel comfortable working with them: More tenured and more senior employees tended to know more people across org lines, while newer or more junior employees knew fewer. This problem was especially acute this year, given those who joined during the pandemic haven’t had as many opportunities to meet coworkers in person as they normally would. In future years, we want to figure out how to incentivize different teams to work together, particularly across Product Development and Business Development.

All in all, we’re proud of what we accomplished this year. But like any good hacker, we look forward to continuing to develop and iterate on what Hack Week looks like in the years to come.
