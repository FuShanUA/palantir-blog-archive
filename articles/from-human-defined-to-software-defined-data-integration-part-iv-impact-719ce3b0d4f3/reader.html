A mistake common to commercial enterprises, government organizations, and SaaS providers alike is to value data as an end in itself, rather than as a means to an end. Data is valuable, of course — leveraging it effectively is one of the greatest challenges facing modern institutions. But, data is not intrinsically valuable.

A distinction made in Kant’s ‘Groundwork of the Metaphysics of Morals’, an end in itself is pursued for its own sake, not as vehicle to achieve something else. Oppositely, a means to an end is valued for its utility in achieving something else; its worth is determined by the outcomes it can unlock.

Time and again, we’ve seen organizations pour millions of dollars into collecting, cataloguing and holding as much data as possible — transactional system data, streaming data, sales data, etc. Their data lakes and warehouses swell with information pooled and pipelined across business units.

However, the value of data is not in the having, but the using. Data is a means to business value, not an end in itself. Without a means of operationalizing this wealth of knowledge against pressing business problems, data becomes an inhibitor, rather than an accelerant.

“You are still adrift while you still think that a means is an end.” ― Idries Shah
