Mission Operations Engineering at Palantir: A software engineer’s perspective

2022-07-07 · Original →

Adil, an engineering manager on the Mission Operations Engineering team at Palantir, talks about his experience and gives advice on how to succeed on the team.

So, what’s Mission Operations Engineering?

The Mission Operations Engineering team operates as a group of Forward Deployed Software Engineers for Palantir itself, tackling business-critical engineering needs across the company. We step in when there are time-sensitive issues (like a backend build pipeline that was recently causing publishing delays for our Marketing teams) and proactively improve processes: for example, we continuously develop and maintain an in-house software platform that automatically assigns support tickets to their appropriate owners several thousand times a day. Our team is also involved in company-level projects, including building data pipelines and visualizations on Palantir’s revenues and expenses within Palantir Foundry for the Finance and Leadership teams. While a Product or Internal Software Engineer might be responsible for the ins and outs of a single complex software system, our team operates on the scale of business outcomes and has a unique view and influence on parts of the company’s direction. This differentiates our team within the software engineering roles at Palantir, both in terms of the demands placed upon us and our reach across the business.

Practically, this means no two weeks on the team are the same. Our engineers are in high demand, so they often need to learn new technologies and acclimate to new organizations quickly; as a result, they have high potential for professional and personal growth. In the last year, our team has worked with the palantir.com Website and Content teams, Finance, International Accounting, Product Reliability Engineering, and Site Reliability Engineering, just to name a few engagements. In each case, we have been given almost complete independence in understanding the problem space and implementing solutions that are easy to understand, maintain, and use.

How did you become a Mission Operations Engineer?

I’ve been an engineering manager on the Mission Operations Engineering team since April 2022, and have served as its Technical Lead since 2019. I originally joined Palantir in 2015 as a Forward Deployed Software Engineer aligned with our Commercial business, with a focus on high-throughput data pipeline design and monitoring for a large financial services client. While the work was challenging and taught me about the world of big data pipeline design and monitoring, I missed using the frontend and backend software engineering skills that I had built up previously in my career.

A year later, the Mission Operations Engineering team was just getting started and was actively seeking full-stack software engineers, so I jumped at the chance to join; I was able to make an immediate impact thanks to my background. I’ve been on this team ever since, and have come to appreciate the high level of trust, vision, and flexibility our team is given to achieve the best outcomes for Palantir. In addition to contributing to a variety of projects on the team, I have mentored many of our current Mission Operations Engineers and interns and hold myself responsible for keeping our team ahead of technical debt and maintenance issues.

My teammates and I all come to the team from varying paths and backgrounds. Some of us are recent college graduates, while others have prior Palantir or industry experience in software engineering, site reliability engineering, data engineering, or product reliability engineering. What brings us all together is strong mission alignment: we succeed when the company does, and so we combine our unique skills and backgrounds to achieve this goal together. We find that the diversity of backgrounds and experiences on the team leads to the creation of robust systems that play to each of our strengths. For example, a software engineering teammate might design the internals of a service and then get a review of its data flows from a data engineering teammate, then run its monitoring and deployment story past a teammate who is experienced in site reliability engineering. This combined strength allows us to assemble cross-functional project groups from within our team, multiplying our breadth and the technical impact we can have in short amounts of time.

What makes a great Mission Operations Engineer?

To succeed as a Mission Operations Engineer, you must first develop a balance between implementing a solution quickly and considering its longer-term stability and maintainability, as we often hand off projects to requesting teams once our solutions have been implemented from end to end. In addition, because we are sometimes asked to pick up or augment another team’s existing work, you must be comfortable working with stakeholders across the company and picking up new programming language skills and tech stacks on demand. On top of this, you must be willing to learn and implement Palantir’s product development software engineering standards so that our applications speak the same language as the rest of the company. Finally, learning how to independently prioritize your day becomes essential, as your time is demanded by several projects at once.

Mission Operations Engineers vary in their day-to-day responsibilities, from those who rapidly iterate on applications within Foundry to demonstrate to our stakeholders on the Finance team, to those who are heads down in their favorite IDE creating the newest features for palantir.com, to others who are building a backend and API for a Business Development team. Though we may be working on projects in pairs or trios, it is our core belief that we all succeed together — our team has a strong culture of knowledge sharing, mentorship, and documentation, all of which are essential when working at a global company like Palantir. Because we are such a fast-paced team, our newest hires are paired with mentors from day one and are empowered to contribute to several initiatives almost immediately, often leveraging all of their existing skills and quickly learning new ones in pursuit of success.

How does the team think about growth?

Growth on the Mission Operations Engineering team can vary depending on an engineer’s strengths and interests, but generally follows a path of increasing responsibilities as an engineer continues to gain experience in several areas. Typically, a Mission Operations Engineer can expect their first few tasks to include working on bug fixes or feature requests in order to familiarize themselves with an existing codebase. Once they’ve begun delivering increasingly complex feature requests at a high-quality standard and have increased their understanding of the product and deployment ecosystem at Palantir, they can begin leading and architecting new projects on their own. Some of our senior engineers lead entire projects, assembling small teams who work with stakeholders to design, develop, test, deploy, and maintain software applications that are critical to Palantir’s success.

Because our team is focused on developing all of its members, we view mentorship and interviewing as well-established paths to growth: by the end of their first year on the team, many engineers begin shadowing on interview circuits, or take up mentoring junior engineers to serve as guideposts for their progress at Palantir.
