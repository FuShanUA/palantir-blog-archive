Making IoT Functional Today — Learnings From The Field

2021-08-30 · Original →

A few years ago, Palantir COO Shyam Sankar penned an op-ed entitled ‘Data is the new snake oil,’ laying bare the strategies employed by software companies to peddle ‘Big Data’ as a panacea for all problems facing the modern enterprise. His point, of course, wasn’t that data is actually snake oil, but that the land rush to capitalize on Big Data’s buzz spawned promises of increasingly fantastical results — promises often disconnected from reality.

In many ways, IoT is the new, new snake oil. I’ll describe why with a few learnings from the field.

IoT isn’t just at the cutting edge; it is the cutting edge, integrating technologies that were mere science fiction a decade ago: edge AI, micro models, sensor fusion, distributed computing, autonomous decision-making, etc. Taken together, these technologies have the potential to unlock operations that are truly revolutionary: engineers identifying machinery defects using AR headsets from home, drones autonomously fixing assets in the field, equipment automatically adjusting to create the golden batch. These use cases are rife in marketing collateral for IoT.

Much like the snake oil claims of Big Data, these visionary IoT workflows are not necessarily false. (In fact, I’ll discuss what the future of IoT looks like in the my next post). But they are disconnected from reality — disconnected from the reality of what enterprises really need from IoT, and what they really need to get there.

Palantir has worked with hundreds of organizations, forward-deploying engineers to problem-solve alongside partners across domains: equipment, plants, workcenters etc. These are real environments that operate with real complexity, real pressure, real KPIs and real problems to solve. Day-to-day users and decision-makers do not need fanciful graphics of what things could look like in 5–10 years time, they need software that works right now. More than that, organizations needs IoT solutions that are operational and democratized. Not a boutique point solution for the few, but a business-first approach for the many.

So, for organizations wanting functional IoT today that actually integrates with day-to-day operations and strategic decision-making, here are three learnings from the field to keep in mind:

IoT is fundamentally a data integration problem.

The core value of IoT data is connectivity to the forefront your operations — i.e. streaming data from thousands of sensors fleet-wide as they take second-by-second pressure readings, so you can be alerted of potential malfunctions in real time. Collecting and analyzing ‘time-series’ data at this petabyte scale has the potential to revolutionize efficiency, but it’s also cumbersome to manage. Traditional databases and analytical applications quickly become saturated with information that may not be relevant for analysis (noise), inhibiting their ability to find and surface valuable insights (signal). This can leave organizations with a mountain of unfiltered, unanalyzed data that continues to grow by the second.

And it’s not just time series data that is difficult to manage, it’s connecting that data and the subsequent analysis to operational workflows. For instance, a root-cause analysis is great; but, when it’s disconnected from a maintenance decision, its rather useless. For this, we need to integrate our time series data with maintenance data, ERP data, shift schedules, lab data, quality data and more to make truly valuable decisions. Hence, we built our IoT suite to seamlessly integrate time series with structured data from other sources, so that the end-users can use time previously wasted on wrangling data and focus it on analysis that really drives results.

Systems have to talk to each other.

All too often, I’ve seen what I like to call the ‘Point Solution Frankenstein’s Monster.’ Organizations have deployed or developed one solution for data integration, one for machine learning, another (or several) for alerting, one for simulations, etc. These solutions might be best-in-class for their specific functions, and that’s great; but, when it comes to integrating and working together, they have to be stitched together with a fragile spiderweb of APIs (not to mention the blood, sweat, and tears of IT). Every time an update comes out for one solution, the web collapses and specialists need to be brought in to re-spin the connections.

To illustrate, suppose I have a ML solution predicting when I should do maintenance. In theory, this sounds great. But, in reality, the ML model is no good unless it can connect to the maintenance system. Maybe a consultant or IT can connect the two via a custom connector. But where does the output go? Likely to a folder or Excel file to (hopefully) be reviewed periodically. While sub-optimal, this is still functional — until, of course, an update to the connector comes out or the IT person leaves and suddenly the system breaks down. The maintenance team reverts back to the usual schedule and the ML solution gathers dust.

Organizations need tools that are operational, not fanciful, tools that actually talk to each other to put the right data in the right hands.

Start small.

With IoT’s dizzying potential, it can be tempting to run before you can walk. If IoT is such a revolutionary technology, why not try integrating it into all applicable business areas right now? I’ve seen a number of organizations try to boil the ocean with IoT — only to end up saddling themselves with huge spend and little impact.

My advice is to start small with a well-defined, high-priority project and grow from there. Once you begin seeing results, ask yourself: “where next? What’s the next logical step from here”’ As you continue to grow and tackle more problems, the speed of execution should increase, enabling more and more use cases to be added to the mix. For example, start with one unit; establish effective monitoring & alerting; then, integrate that with an operational maintenance workflow. Now, start doing predictive maintenance and have those insights feed into the established alerting workflow. Scale to several units. Then the plant; then several plants.

SaaS platforms are often an ideal match for IoT workflows because they enable precisely this flexibility and scalability. You can start small, but you can also easily roll out to other areas of your business, so that all your IoT projects are seamlessly integrated. The sample growth pattern I laid out above might require three, five, or even ten different point solutions — introducing huge strain on IT to maintain, not to mention spend for the business. Meanwhile, SaaS platforms such as ours can grow at pace — all the while building an integrated, common data foundation and fostering seamless collaboration between users.

Concluding thoughts

IoT can be the new oil, fueling the future of of your enterprise, or it can be the new snake oil, promising a cure-all and delivering a cure-none. It all depends on how you implement it.

  • Get the data integration right — time series data is not valuable in isolation
  • Opt for platforms and solutions that work seamlessly together and are easy for IT to maintain
  • Start small and scale — don’t try and do everything at once

Predictive maintenance, real-time alerting, the golden-batch and optimized simulations are all achievable today; however, without the core foundations outlined above, these workflows will not prove fruitful.
